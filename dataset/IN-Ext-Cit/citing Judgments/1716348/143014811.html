Calcutta High Court (Appellete Side)
Amiya Mukherjee vs State Of West Bengal & Another on 11 June, 2014
Author: Ashim Kumar Roy

1 Form J (1) IN THE HIGH COURT AT CACUTTA (Criminal Jurisdiction) Appellate Side Present:

The Hon'ble Justice Ashim Kumar Roy C. R. R. No. 1558 of 2005 Amiya Mukherjee Versus State of West Bengal & Another For the Petitioner Mr. Kaushik Chatteree, For the State : Mr. Dipanjan Chatterjee For the De Facto Complainant : Mr. Kaushik Gupta, Mr. Arijit Bhusan Bagchi Heard on: 16th April, 2014.

Judgement on: 11.6.2014.

Ashim Kumar Roy, J.:

Invoking section 482 of the Criminal Procedure Code the petitioner, who happens to be the husband of the opposite party no. 2/wife has approached this court for quashing of the charge sheet relating to the offences punishable under sections 498A/494/34 of the Indian Penal Code.

The grounds on which the quashing has been sought for are as follows :

(i) Earlier the petitioner was tried on a charge under section 494 of the Indian Penal Code and acquitted. The said order of acquittal was never challenged in any higher forum and has reached its finality. Therefore, his prosecution once again for 2 the self-same offence is not legally permissible both under CrPC and Constitution.

(ii) Out of sheer mala fide suppressing the petitioner's previous trial for the self-same offence under section 494 of the Indian Penal Code and his acquittal, an FIR has been lodged and the impugned charge sheet is an outcome thereof. Thus the very initiation of the case is bad in law.

(iii) In the previous trial, the court, on scrutinizing the evidence, came to a definite finding that the opposite party wife left her matrimonial home in June, 1983, which she admitted in her cross-examination and it was never claimed that thereafter she ever returned to her matrimonial home. Therefore, the torture, if any, perpetrated upon her must be before she left her matrimonial home and the F. I. R. which was lodged on 19th April, 2005 nearly after about 23 years for an offence under section 498A of the Indian Penal Code and the impugned charge sheet is hit by the provisions of section 468 CrPC.

(iv) In connection with a maintenance proceeding under section 125 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, similar was the evidence of the opposite party wife that she left her matrimonial home in February, 1983 and there was no claim that she again returned. Therefore, if she was at all subjected to any torture that must be before February, 1983.

(v) The taking of cognizance on a police report relating to an offence punishable under section 494 IPC, is absolutely without jurisdiction, on the face of the specific statutory bar contained in section 198 CrPC.

The following judgments have been relied upon from the side of the petitioner:

(1) M. Saravana Porselvi vs. A. R. Chandrasekhar alias Parthiban and Others reported in (2009) 1 SCC (Cri) 404. (2) MCD vs. State of Delhi & Another reported in 2005 SCC (Cri) 1322.
(3) G. Narasimhan, G. Kasturi and K. Gopalan vs. T. V. Chokkappa reported in 1972 SCC (Cri) 777. (4) Ashwin Nanubhai Vyas vs. State of Maharashtra & Another reported in (1967) 1 SCR 807.

On the other hand the learned counsel for the State produced the case diary and submitted that so far as the materials collected during 3 investigation, there is no clear evidence as to when she left her matrimonial home allegedly being driven out by her in-laws except the allegation that she was driven out long back. He further submitted that the order passed in connection with a proceeding under section 125 of the Code of Criminal Procedure is also with the case diary. According to the evidence recorded therein, she was driven out from her matrimonial home in February, 1983. He further submitted that she was also examined under section 161 of the Code of Criminal Procedure but there also she never disclosed when she was actually driven out, except, disclosing for the first time, the name of the lady Mita Mukherjee, whom, her husband, married for the second time. He then added that in the first trial the lady whom the petitioner married was described as Bula Mukherjee @ Roy. It is further pointed out that in connection with the present case the alleged second wife applied for anticipatory bail where she described herself as Mita Mukherjee alias Bula Roy and both of them are the same persons. Lastly he submitted only materials as regards to her being driven out from her matrimonial home was in February 1983.

On the other hand the learned advocate for the wife/opposite party submitted that there is no legal impediment in police investigation, where the FIR discloses both the offences punishable under section 498A and 494 of the Indian Penal Code inasmuch as one of the offences is cognizable. He then added that there is also no bar to take cognizance of an offence punishable under section 494 of the Indian Penal Code on a police report by the court.

In support of his submissions reliance has been placed by the learned counsel for the wife in the case of Usha Ben vs. Kishor Bhai Chunilal Talapada & Others reported in 2012 Criminal Law Journal 2234.

Heard the learned counsel for the parties and perused the case diary containing the charge sheeted materials.

In the case of G. Narashimham vs. T. V. Chokkappa (Supra) and Ashwin Nanubhai Vyas vs. State of Maharashtra and Another (supra) two different three Judges Bench of the Apex Court, uniformly held that the provisions of section 198 of the Code of Criminal Procedure is mandatory and the 4 prohibition contained therein completely restricts the power of the court in taking cognizance of any such offence referred in section 198 of the Code of Criminal Procedure. Therefore, although it may be permissible for the police to undertake investigation where the FIR relates to both the offences one punishable under section 498A IPC, the other under Section 494 IPC, while one is cognizable and the other is non-cognizable. However the court is not legally empowered to take cognizance of an offence punishable under section 494 of the Indian Penal Code on a police report on the face of the mandate of section 198 of the Code of Criminal Procedure. Furthermore, there is no disclosure that previously the petitioner was tried for an offence under section 494 of the Indian Penal Code and was acquitted. There is also neither any disclosure as to the identity of the alleged second wife in the FIR nor in the 161 statement of the de facto complainant wife. There is also no claim that both the women were different. However, it is pointed out to this court from the side of the State that while in the previous trial the second wife was described as Bula Mukherjee alias Bula Roy but in the instant case she has been described as Mita Mukherjee. According to the learned counsel for the State both of them are same person. From the evidentiary material collected during investigation there is no clear picture who was the lady, whom the present petitioner married for the second time. It is also not the allegation in the present complaint that he married for the second time another lady who was not referred in the first trial. The acquittal of the petitioner for an offence punishable under Section 494 IPC has also not been disputed from the side of the defacto complainant.

Now, coming to the offence punishable under section 498A of the Indian Penal Code, I find so far as the charge sheeted materials are concerned, it is an admitted position that she was driven out from her matrimonial home about 23 years back and there is no material forthcoming that thereafter she again returned there. Therefore, the case made out from the side of the petitioner and endorsed by the learned counsel for the State that if at all she was subjected to torture, that was before she left her matrimonial home in the year 1983, in absence of anything more, is quite justified. Accordingly, the impugned charge 5 sheet for an offence punishable under section 498A of the Indian Penal Code on the basis of an FIR lodged in the year 2005 is clearly hit by the prohibition contained in section 468 CrPC.

For the reasons stated above, in my opinion, a case for quashing has certainly been made out and accordingly the impugned proceeding stands quashed.

Urgent photostat certified copy of the order, if applied for, be supplied to the learned advocates for both the sides within a week from the date of making such application.

(Ashim Kumar Roy, J.)
