This Interim Application seeks temporary injunction in respect of disparagement of the Plaintif's goods in particular and infringement of the Plaintif's registered trademark in general.

2. The Plaintif claims to be a manufacturer and marketer of well-known and established household brands such as 'Nihar' 'Parachute', 'Livon'. These brands, which are registered trademarks of the Plaintif, have been used for Laxmi Pg 1 of 16 ::: Uploaded on - 20/03/2020 ::: Downloaded on - 09/06/2020 14:00:54 ::: 2 IA 1-19 in COMIPL 1262-2019.odt marketing of various hair and skin care products, health foods, etc. We are concerned in the present case particularly with the Plaintif's trademark 'NIHAR', which the Plaintif uses, with or without addition/s to it, for marketing its Amla Hair Oil products('Nihar range of trademarks'). The Plaintif claims to be a market leader (by sales in volume). According to the Plaintif, about 42.6% of Amla Hair Oil sold in the market comes under the Plaintif's Nihar range of trademarks. The Plaintif's case is not only that the product, namely, Amla Hair Oil, is marketed under NIHAR range of trademarks but the bottles in which the product is sold come in particular size, shape, features, contours and confguration. It is submitted that these elements are distinctive and unique to the product marketed by the Plaintif. It is submitted that the Plaintif has also designed distinctive labels for NIHAR range of Amla Hair Oil to go with the particular shape and size of the bottles referred to above. The Plaintif has obtained registration for the particular label, which is used on its bottles with the same shape. The Plaintif's case is that it has acquired great reputation and goodwill in NIHAR range of products, all of which are Amla Hair Oil. The reputation and goodwill is not only in the trademark NIHAR, Laxmi Pg 2 of 16 ::: Uploaded on - 20/03/2020 ::: Downloaded on - 09/06/2020 14:00:54 ::: 2 IA 1-19 in COMIPL 1262-2019.odt with or without additional word/s, but also in the distinctive shape, size and contours of the bottles used for marketing the goods. The Plaintif's grievance is that the Defendant, who is a rival manufacturer and marketer of Amla Hair Oil, advertises its goods in newspapers and other media comparing them with other rival products, terming them as 'cheap oil' or in Hindi 'sasta tel' which are said to cause hair loss or breakage; the Defendant thereby disparages these latter products as bad quality products. The Plaintif's case is that these advertisements create an unmistakable impression that the rival products referred to therein, which are described as cheap, are the Plaintif's products. It is submitted that the Plaintif's products are cheaper than the Defendant's. It is submitted that the distinctive shape, size and contours of the bottles, in which the Plaintif markets its products, are to be found in the bottle of 'cheap oil' displayed in the advertisement and which is said to be harmful to hair, causing hair fall or breakage. The Plaintif's case is that these advertisements are issued with a malicious intent to slander or disparage the Plaintif's NIHAR range of Amla Hair Oil and gain unjustly by promoting the Defendant's own hair oil by denigrating or Laxmi Pg 3 of 16 ::: Uploaded on - 20/03/2020 ::: Downloaded on - 09/06/2020 14:00:54 ::: 2 IA 1-19 in COMIPL 1262-2019.odt showing in a poor light the Plaintif's product. The case, in short, is a case of injurious falsehood and disparagement of goods.

3. As this Court has noted in its ad-interim order in this matter passed on 4 December, 2019 and as afrmed by the Defendant in its afdavit in reply dated 21 January, 2020, the Defendant does not any more use the original advertisements, which are complained of in the plaint, but has changed the shape, size, contours, etc. of the rival bottle of 'cheap oil', which is now shown in the advertisement for comparison with the Defendant's product. The Defendant has placed sample advertisements on record as Exhibits 'A1' and 'A2' to its afdavit in reply. Rival cases of the parties are, thus, to be assessed not in the light of the original ofending ads, which form part of the Plaint, but in the context of the new ads now proposed by the Defendant as indicated in its afdavit in reply to the Interim Application.

4. Mr. Tulzapurkar, learned Senior Counsel appearing for the Plaintif, submits that the Hindi words 'sasta tel' or the Laxmi Pg 4 of 16 ::: Uploaded on - 20/03/2020 ::: Downloaded on - 09/06/2020 14:00:54 ::: 2 IA 1-19 in COMIPL 1262-2019.odt English words 'cheap oil' used respectively in Hindi and English versions of the new advertisements nevertheless suggest a clear reference to the Plaintif's products. Learned Counsel submits that the intention of the Defendant was always to compare its product with the rival product of the Plaintif. In support of this submission, it is submitted that originally, the shape, size and contours of the bottle used in the ofending advertisements for disparaging goods were unmistakably of the Plaintif's bottles. Learned Counsel submits that 'with prejudice' ofer made by the Defendant at the time of the ad-interim application and the suggestion now made in its afdavit in reply to the Interim Application clearly amount to an admission on its part that the original bottle used in the disparaging advertisements was meant to be the Plaintif's bottle. Learned Counsel also submits that the test reports, which are produced with the original afdavit of the Defendant (afdavit dated 3 December, 2019), to oppose the ad-interim application, only indicate tests carried out for comparing the two products, that is to say, the Plaintif's Amla Hair Oil and Defendant's Amal Hair Oil. Learned Counsel submits that having regard to the dates of these test reports, it is apparent that they were Laxmi Pg 5 of 16 ::: Uploaded on - 20/03/2020 ::: Downloaded on - 09/06/2020 14:00:54 ::: 2 IA 1-19 in COMIPL 1262-2019.odt obtained even prior to the Plaintif's complaint about disparaging advertising. Learned Counsel submits that this also underscores the Defendant's intention to denigrate or disparage the Plaintif's goods in its advertisements.

5. I am afraid none of the circumstances cited by Mr. Tulzapurkar supports his submission in any real sense. Making of a 'with prejudice' ofer at the ad-interim stage, followed by adoption and publication of new advertisements, does not imply any admission on the Defendant's part that the original product used in the so-called disparaging advertisements was the Plaintif's product. It may, on the other hand, be suggested that what this indicates is that the Defendant is not interested in disparaging any particular product, let alone the Plaintif's Amla Hair Oil. The shape, size and contours of the bottle of rival product shown for disparagement in the ofending advertisements do not matter; they do not seem to be of any material importance. The sense conveyed in the advertisement, as submitted by learned Counsel for the Defendant, may well be of cautioning of discerning purchasers that there may be products of a cheap variety, assuming that Laxmi Pg 6 of 16 ::: Uploaded on - 20/03/2020 ::: Downloaded on - 09/06/2020 14:00:54 ::: 2 IA 1-19 in COMIPL 1262-2019.odt the word 'cheap' here implies nothing but a product of lesser price; they, however, may not be good for hair; they may lead to hair fall or hair breakage. The customer is asked to beware of these products and informed about the superior quality of the Defendant's product in comparison. In keeping with this broad sense, the Defendant has readily ofered to change the shape, size and contours of the bottle of disparaged product in the advertisements. The above may be an equally ftting explanation for the conduct of the Defendant referred to by learned Counsel for the Plaintif. There is no admission here that the original product shown in the advertisement for comparing with the Defendant's product was the Plaintif's bottle of Amla Hair Oil.

6. Even the other circumstance cited by Mr. Tulzapurkar, namely, the test reports produced with the afdavit in reply to the ad-interim application showing comparison only between the Plaintif's and the Defendant's rival products, does not support the Plaintif's case. That may be due to several factors as submitted by Mr. Dhond, learned Senior Counsel appearing for the Defendant. It may well be Laxmi Pg 7 of 16 ::: Uploaded on - 20/03/2020 ::: Downloaded on - 09/06/2020 14:00:54 ::: 2 IA 1-19 in COMIPL 1262-2019.odt because the Defendant would have anticipated a legal action on the part of the Plaintif. Indeed, the Defendant had even proceeded to fle a caveat to protect itself from any such action. The parties have been in Court already on the basis of rival promotion campaigns. It may well be that the Defendant was preparing a ground for defending any such anticipated action. It is by no means an admission on its part that the idea behind the advertising campaign was to denigrate or disparage the Plaintif's goods.

7. Mr. Tulzapurkar, alternatively, submits that even if the product named in the disparaging advertisement may not be seen as the Plaintif's product, going either by the trademark or the shape, size or contours of the bottle, so long as the disparagement is of all products which are cheaper in terms of price, it is a disparagement of a whole class of goods, namely, cheaper goods, and in as much as the Plaintif's goods fall within this class, there is a cause of action unto the Plaintif. Mr. Dhond counters this submission by suggesting that the word 'cheap' is really used here in the sense of an inferior product and not necessarily a product of lesser price. Mr. Tulzapurkar Laxmi Pg 8 of 16 ::: Uploaded on - 20/03/2020 ::: Downloaded on - 09/06/2020 14:00:54 ::: 2 IA 1-19 in COMIPL 1262-2019.odt rejoins by submitting that even if the words 'cheap oil', or in Hindi 'sasta tel', may imply products both of an inferior grade as suggested by the Defendant as also products of less price as is the Plaintif's case, the words used are clearly tricky and capable of conveying the meaning of a cheaper variety of oil in terms of price to the consumers or the public and if that is so, it is nothing but a case of disparagement of a whole class of goods and since the Plaintif's product falls within this class, there is a cause of action. Learned Counsel relies on the Supreme Court decision in the case of Lakhanpal National Ltd. Vs. MRTP Commission1 and a Division Bench decision of our Court in Gujarat Co-operative Milk Marketing Federation Limited Vs. Hindustan Unilever Limited 2 in support of his submission.

8. First of all, this Court cannot persuade itself to believe that in substance, the impression sought to be conveyed to an ordinary man on the street or buyer of the goods in question is that all products of 'Amla Hair Oil', which are priced lesser than the Plaintif's 'Amla Hair Oil', are inferior 1 1989 SCC (3) 251 2 MANU/MH/3442/2018 Laxmi Pg 9 of 16 ::: Uploaded on - 20/03/2020 ::: Downloaded on - 09/06/2020 14:00:54 ::: 2 IA 1-19 in COMIPL 1262-2019.odt in quality or that they lead to hair fall or hair breakage. That certainly, in my opinion, is not the impression meant to be conveyed or is likely to be conveyed to a reasonable man on the street or an ordinar/y consumer of the subject goods. The Defendant, of course, as I have noted above, may be said to have meant to use the words 'cheap oil' or 'sasta tel' as suggestive of lesser price and not necessarily of inferior quality. It is, certainly arguable, as Mr. Tulzapurkar suggests, that the word here conveys both meanings; it may, in fact, in that sense have been used tongue in cheek; but it, by no means, suggests that what the advertisement disparages are products of lesser price as a class. What the advertisement, taken at its plain face value, conveys is that there could be products which are cheap (that is, of lesser price), but the consumers better beware - these might be cheap, not just in terms of price, but even in terms of quality; these might yet be harmful and lead to conditions such as hair fall or hair breakage. In comparison, the Plaintif's products are shown as 'True Amla', that is to say, of a purer variety. There is no disparagement in this of the whole range of cheaper (in terms of price) variety of amla hair oil generally, much less of any one product in particular, or, for Laxmi Pg 10 of 16 ::: Uploaded on - 20/03/2020 ::: Downloaded on - 09/06/2020 14:00:54 ::: 2 IA 1-19 in COMIPL 1262-2019.odt that matter, the Plaintif's amla hair oil. All that this suggests is that the Defendant, in its advertisements, calls upon consumers to pay more attention to quality rather than go merely by price. The disparagement, in other words, if at all there is any, is of products, which are 'cheap', not just in terms of price, but also of quality. It may well be that both senses of the word 'cheap' or 'sasta' are invoked in the present case to convey the above. Ambivalence such as this, refected in the copy, actually lends literary merit or artistic value or adds punch to the advertisement. There is no suggestion here, as Mr. Tulzapurkar suggests, that all products of lesser price are generally inferior, much less that the Plaintif's product in particular is inferior.

9. The facts in Gujarat Co-operative Milk Marketing Federation Ltd. (supra) decided by the Division Bench of our court are entirely distinguishable. That was a case where the plaintif was manufacturing frozen desserts. The impugned television commercials produced by the defendants claimed that ice creams marketed by the defendants under its brand name 'Amul' were produced by using 100% milk, whereas Laxmi Pg 11 of 16 ::: Uploaded on - 20/03/2020 ::: Downloaded on - 09/06/2020 14:00:54 ::: 2 IA 1-19 in COMIPL 1262-2019.odt frozen desserts were manufactured by using vanaspati tel, which is hydrogenated vegetable oil, considered generally to be unhealthy in Indian society. It was submitted by the Plaintif that for preparing its frozen desserts, the Plaintif was not using vanaspati in its products, but edible vegetable oil. It was the grievance of the Plaintif that the impugned television commercials denigrated frozen desserts as a class and on a misrepresentation that they contained 100% vanaspati. The Division Bench accepted the plaintif's case, holding that it had no manner of doubt that the particular TV commercials complained of had the efect of disparaging frozen desserts in general and dissuading customers from using them and the plaintif's product being a frozen dessert, the commercials amounted to disparagement of the plaintif's product. Ramchandran7 decided by the High Courts of Bombay, Delhi, Madras and Calcutta.

10. In Godrej Consumer Products Ltd., a learned Single Judge of our court (B.R. Gavai, J., as the learned Judge then was) had referred to the case of Dabur India Ltd. (supra). In Dabur India Ltd., the sum and substance of the impugned advertisement was said to evoke the memory of 'Lal Dant Manjan Powder' marketed by the Plaintif. Though the product name of the plaintif was not mentioned it it, the red container and the bottle used in the advertisement was identical with the one used by the plaintif. The court had observed in Dabur India Ltd. that the advertisement campaign in visual media had an immediate impact on the minds of viewers as well as possible purchasers of the deleterious efect of red powder used for cleaning teeth, which one could identify with the plaintif's product. The court observed that no one could disparage a class or genre of products, within which the plaintif's product fell, though the 6 2008 (38) PTC 37 (Mad) 7 1999 (19) PTC 741 Laxmi Pg 13 of 16 ::: Uploaded on - 20/03/2020 ::: Downloaded on - 09/06/2020 14:00:54 ::: 2 IA 1-19 in COMIPL 1262-2019.odt latter's product was not specifcally identifed for such disparagement. Delhi High Court's decision in Reckit Benckiser (India) Ltd. (supra) was also rendered in the context of the facts of that case where, though the name of the soap was not indicated for disparagement, the colours, the contours and the overall shape of the soap used in the advertisement clearly evoked the memory of the plaintif's product and these elements, coupled with the particular colour of packaging material used by the adversary in its commercial, gave a clear impression that the bar of soap shown in the advertisement, going by its particular colour, shape and contours and the wrapper or packaging used for it, was the plaintif's soap. The court considered that there could be soaps of the same colour, soaps of the same shape and soaps packaged in the same colour wrappers, but the plaintif's was the only soap with a combination of all three elements, that is to say, the particular colour (orange), the particular shape (curved oval) and the particular colour of packaging (green). The court had, in the premises, no manner of doubt that the particular bar of soap disparaged in the advertisement referred to the plaintif's soap and none else. In Annamalayar Laxmi Pg 14 of 16 ::: Uploaded on - 20/03/2020 ::: Downloaded on - 09/06/2020 14:00:54 ::: 2 IA 1-19 in COMIPL 1262-2019.odt Agencies's case, the plaintif's product, namely, "Parachute Coconut Oil", was bottled in blue colour bottles, whereas the defendant's product was bottled in green colour bottles. Every suggestion was made by the defendant in the impugned advertisement for denigrating the product coming in blue bottle. In these facts, Madras High Court observed that the advertisement clearly made a suggestion and sent a message to television viewers and buyers of coconut oil that blue bottle products were inferior to the green bottle products, which were marketed by the defendant and that accordingly, there was a case of colourable disparagement. The case of Reckitt & Colman of India Ltd. (supra) decided by Calcutta High Court also proceeded on a more or less similar footing. The Plaintif's product in that case, marketed under the trade mark 'Robin Blue', was disparaged by the defendant in its advertisement, which used the word 'blue' for disparaging the product. The Court was of the view that though the word used for depicting the product was 'Blue', it was difcult to hold that the reference was not to 'Robin Blue'. The facts of all these cases are clearly distinguishable from the facts of the present case. The shape, size, colour or contours of the bottle of disparaged product Laxmi Pg 15 of 16 ::: Uploaded on - 20/03/2020 ::: Downloaded on - 09/06/2020 14:00:54 ::: 2 IA 1-19 in COMIPL 1262-2019.odt shown in the impugned advertisement cannot be said to be evoking, in any real sense, the memory of the plaintif's product. Even, for that matter, the reference to 'Cheap Amla Hair Oil' cannot be said to evoke the memory of the Plaintif's product. As I have noted above, it is difcult to hold that what are disparaged in the impugned advertisement are lesser price products as a class. So also, the Plaintif has not shown at this prima facie stage that the phrases 'cheap hair oil' or 'sasta tel' used in the advertisement were indeed an idea associated solely with the plaintif's product. It is difcult to say, on the basis of materials produced before the court, that the reference to 'cheap hair oil' or 'sasta tail' evokes the memory of the plaintif's hair oil.

11. Accordingly, there is no merit in the interim application. The interim application is dismissed.
