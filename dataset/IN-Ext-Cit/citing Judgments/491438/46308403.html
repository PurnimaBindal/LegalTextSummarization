Before proceeding further I wish to place on record that Sections 227 and 228, Cr.P.C., enjoin upon the trial court a duty to hear the parties, viz. the prosecution and the defence, and consider the record available before it to find out what offence, if any, is shown to have been committed by the accused and then Section 354, Cr.P.C., requires that every judgment, as the one in case in hand is, shall, inter alia, "contain the point or points for determination, the decision thereon and the reasons for the decision". This, in my view, is not a mere procedural formality. It, rather, aids the trial court to identify the areas of discord between the prosecution and the defence and, thereby facilitates the court to attempt at a resolution of those points so as to reach a just conclusion. In his statement, Exhibit PA, before the Investigating Officer and while appearing as PW1 before the trial court, the injured PW1-Gian Singh has stated in so many words that the appellants ambushed him on the way and inflicted injuries on his person with an iron rod and a hockey stick, with an intention to kill him. Out of these, injury Nos. 3, 24, and 25 were found to be grievous in nature. Injury Nos. 6 and 12 are on the chest and injury Nos. 16 and 17 are on back side of the chest, injury No. 30 is on the forehead and injury No. 9 is on the spine. A glance across the medico legal report, Exhibit PB, and pictorial diagrams, Exhibit PB/1, PB/2, and PB/3, reveals that no part of the body of the victim is left unhurt in the occurrence. From the circumstances a clear cut case punishable under Section 307, IPC, is made out. What has been said and discussed in the immediately preceding paragraph also demolishes argument of the learned counsel for the appellants that the injuries were on non-vital parts of the body, were caused by blunt weapon(s) and were not dangerous to life individually or collectively and, as such, the case goes out of the purview of Section 308, IPC. A witness may be called "interested" only when he or she derives some benefit from the result of a litigation; in the decree in a civil case, or in seeing an accused person punished. A witness who is a natural one and is the only possible eyewitness in the circumstances of a case cannot be said to be "interested". Defence has not been able to bring any material on record, either in the cross- examination of the witnesses of prosecution or by leading evidence in defence, to show that PW3-Sujan Singh either has derived or is likely to derive any monetary or other benefit for himself or for any one whom he may be interested in, by incarceration of the appellants or that he had an animus hostile to them. His presence on the spot, at the time of the occurrence, was quite natural and he has not only been able to pass the litmus test of cross-examination successfully, but also his testimony is in consonance with that of injured PW1-Gian Singh, who being the injured his testimony deserves a special status in law. This is as a consequence of the fact that the injury to the witness is an inbuilt guarantee of his presence at the scene of the crime and because the witness will not want to let his actual assailant go unpunished merely to falsely implicate a third party for the commission of the offence. It cannot even be imagined that he would inflict 31 injuries, including three grievous injuries, on his body only to falsely implicate the appellants and there are no grounds, much less strong grounds, such as major contradictions and discrepancies for rejection of his evidence. Non examination of someone from the shops near to the place of occurrence is rendered inconsequential in view of the occurrence having been proved by injured PW1-Gian Singh and his evidence having been corroborated by that of PW3-Sujan Singh and the medical evidence because Section 134 of Indian Evidence Act, 1872, does not lay down that in proof of a particular fact a particular number of witnesses are required to be examined. Even otherwise, it is a matter of common knowledge that people do not come forward to depose in respect of an occurrence because they feel that the occurrence is a dispute between two individuals or parties and their deposition in favour of either of them is bound to incur wrath of the other and they would be required to make rounds of the court unnecessarily. It should suffice to refer to two, out of many, judgments recognizing relictance of the general public to depose as witnesses in criminal cases. In Appabhai V. State of Gujarat, AIR 1988 SC 696, the Hon'ble Supreme Court of India observed as under:

"Experience reminds us that civilized people are generally insensitive when a crime is committed even in their presence. They withdraw from the victim and the vigilante. They keep themselves away from the Court unless it is inevitable. They think that crime like civil dispute is between two individuals or parties and they should not involve themselves. This kind of Virendra singh adhikari 2014.02.19 09:23 I attest to the accuracy and integrity of this document High Court Chandigarh CRA-S No.163-SB of 2003 -14- apathy of general public is indeed unfortunate, but it there everywhere whether in village life, towns or cities. One cannot ignore this handicap with which the investigating agency has to discharge its duties. The court, therefore, instead of doubting the prosecution for want of independent witness must consider the broad spectrum of the prosecution version and then search for the nugget of truth with due regard o probability, if any, suggested by the accused."

Similarly in Sarwan Singh V. State of Punjab, 2002(4) RCR(Crl.) 471, it was held as follows:

".... It is known fact that independent persons are reluctant to be witnesses or to assist the investigation. Reasons are not far to seek. Firstly, in cases where injured witnesses or the close relatives of the deceased are under constant threat and they dare not depose the truth before the Court, independent witnesses believe that their safety is not guaranteed. That belief cannot be said to be without any substance. Another reason may be the delay in recording the evidence of independent witnesses and repeated adjournments in the Court. In any case, if independent persons are not willing to cooperate with the investigation, the prosecution cannot be blamed and it cannot be a ground for rejecting the evidence of injured witnesses."

When the occurrence has been proved beyond reasonable doubt the question of delay pales into insignificance. Still, it is seen that the injured PW1-Gian Singh received thirty one (31) injuries out which three were grievous in nature, in the occurrence which took place at or around 01.30 p.m. on 15.06.1999. No one from his family was around. PW3-Sujan Singh, on seeing him being given injuries by the appellants, reached the spot and after the Virendra singh adhikari 2014.02.19 09:23 I attest to the accuracy and integrity of this document High Court Chandigarh CRA-S No.163-SB of 2003 -15- assailants fled the scene, informed complainant's son Amandeep Singh and both of them took the complainant to Civil Hospital, Mukerian. Even otherwise, "Delay in lodging the FIR cannot be used as a ritualistic formula for doubting the prosecution case and discarding the same solely on the ground of delay in lodging the first information report. Delay has the effect of putting the Court on its guard to search if any plausible explanation has been offered for the delay, and if offered, whether it is satisfactory or not. If the prosecution fails to satisfactorily explain the delay and there is possibility of embellishment in the prosecution version on account of such delay, the delay would be fatal to the prosecution. However, if the delay is explained to the satisfaction of the Court, the delay cannot by itself be a ground for disbelieving and discarding the entire prosecution case.", ruled the Hon'ble Supreme Court in State of Himachal Pradesh v. Gian Chand, 2001 (6) SCC 71.
