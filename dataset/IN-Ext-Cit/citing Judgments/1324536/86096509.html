                         JUDGMENT

This appeal by the sole accused is directed against the judgment and order dated 15.04.2005 passed by the Fast Track Court III, Kolar in S.C.No.146/2004, convicting him for the offence punishable under Section 302 IPC and sentencing him to undergo imprisonment for life and also to pay fine of Rs.1,000/- for the said offence.

2. The case of the prosecution in brief is as under: Deceased Narayanaswamy is the maternal uncle of the appellant / accused. They were resident of Keelukuppa Village in Bangarpet Taluk, and were coolies by profession. On 28.04.2003 at about 9.00 p.m. the deceased Narayanaswamy was sleeping inside the house, at that time the accused came near house of the deceased, and started 3 kicking the door; that on the deceased opening the door, the accused picked up quarrel with him with regard to the payment of coolie / wages; that during the quarrel the accused picked up wooden reaper from the heap of firewood stored by the side of the house, assaulted the deceased on the head repeatedly as a result of which the deceased sustained bleeding injuries and met instantaneous death. P.W.14 Chinna Guruva, the neighbour of deceased and an eye witness to the incident of assault on deceased, immediately telephoned to the jurisdictional police, and waited at the scene of occurrence for the arrival of the jurisdictional police. However, the police did not arrive at the spot, therefore, P.W.14 himself rushed to the police station and lodged a report as per Ex.P-6 at about 10.45 p.m., on the same day based on which, the police registered the case and took up investigation. During the investigation the Investigating Officer visited the scene of occurrence, conducted inquest over the dead body, and later sent the dead body to the hospital for post mortem examination. Thereafter, Investigating Officer arrested the accused and 4 recovered the weapon used for the commission of the offence at the instance of accused, and recorded statements of witnesses.

3. On completion of investigation, police laid the charge sheet. When accused was produced before the magistrate, he was remanded to the judicial custody and thereafter, he was released on bail on 21.10.2003. On committal of case, the appellant / accused appeared before the Learned Sessions Judge, pleaded not guilty for the charge leveled against him and claimed to be tried. The prosecution in order to bring home the guilty of the accused, examined PWs 1 to 19 and relied on documentary evidence marked as Exs.P-1 to P-15 and material objects MOs 1 to 9. During examination under Section 313 of Cr.P.C. by the learned Sessions Judge, the accused denied all the incriminating circumstances appearing against him in the evidence of the prosecution witnesses. However, he did not chose to lead any defence evidence. The defence of the accused was one of total denial and that of false implication. It was his further 5 defence that the deceased under intoxication of liquor fell into the drainage and sustained injuries, as a result of which died on the spot and that he was not responsible for the death of deceased.

4. The learned Sessions Judge after hearing both the sides and on appreciation of materials on record, by the judgment under appeal held that the prosecution has proved that the death of the deceased was homicidal and by placing reliance on the evidence of P.W.14, held that the prosecution has proved beyond reasonable doubt the complicity of the accused for the homicidal death of deceased. Having regard to the fact that the deceased had sustained four head injuries resulting in crushing of skull bones, the learned Sessions Judge came to the conclusion that the act committed by the accused amounted to murder punishable under section 302 of IPC. Learned Sessions Judge rejected the argument of the defence that the act committed by the accused would be culpable homicide not amounting to murder punishable under Section 304 of IPC. Consequently, 6 learned Sessions Judge convicted the appellant/accused for the offence punishable under Section 302 of IPC and sentenced him to undergo imprisonment for life and also to pay fine. Aggrieved by the said judgment of conviction and order of sentence, the appellant is before this Court.

5. We have heard Sri G.M.Sreenivasa Reddy, for Sri Y.R.Sadasiva Reddy, learned counsel appearing for the appellant / accused and Sri K.R.Keshavamurthy, learned Addl. SPP appearing for respondent - State. Perused the records secured from the Trial Court as well as the judgment under appeal.

6. Sri Sreenivasa Reddy, learned counsel appearing for the appellant / accused contended that the judgment under appeal suffers from perversity and illegality for the reasons that the learned Sessions Judge has failed to consider that the evidence of P.W.14 is not cogent and consistent as such not worthy of acceptance. He further contended that from the evidence of P.W.14 it is clear that he cannot be 7 considered as an eye witness and in the absence of any other evidence to connect the accused to the death of deceased, the learned Sessions Judge has committed grave error in holding the appellant guilty of the charge leveled as such the judgment under appeal is perverse and is liable to be set aside and the accused is entitled to be acquitted. In the alternative the learned counsel for the appellant contended that the alleged act by the accused would not amount to murder punishable under Section 302 of IPC, but it would be culpable homicide not amounting to murder punishable under Part II of Section 304 IPC. According to the learned counsel the circumstances brought on record would indicate that there was no preparation on the part of the accused to commit murder nor the appellant had any intention to kill the deceased, therefore, the learned Sessions Judge is not justified in convicting the appellant for the offence punishable under Section 302 IPC and on other hand, the learned counsel for appellant submits that the alleged act, at best may fall under Part II of Section 304 of 8 IPC. Therefore, he sought for modification of the judgment of conviction, and order of sentence.

8. In support of his arguments the learned counsel for the appellant placed reliance on the following decisions:

1. Order dated 03.11.2008 passed by this Court in Crl.A.No.555/2002: State of Karnataka vs. Sundara Naika
2. 2009 AIR SCW 6077: Satish Narayan Sawant vs. State of Goa.

3. ILR 2013 KAR 2565: Ankush Shivaji Gaikwad vs. State of Maharashtra

9. Per contra, Sri K.R.Keshavamurthy, learned Addl. SPP contended that the learned Sessions Judge on proper assessment of the oral and documentary evidence and by assigning cogent reasons, has found that the evidence of P.W.14 is consistent and cogent and that there is no circumstance to discard the testimony of P.W.14. He further contended that the presence of P.W.14 at the scene of occurrence being quite natural, since he is resident of the house opposite to the house of deceased, therefore, he is a 9 natural witness. He further contended that the evidence of P.W.14 clearly establishes that there was sufficient light at the place of occurrence for him to witness the incident and the overtact on the part of accused, as such the learned Sessions Judge is justified in accepting the evidence of P.W.14. He further contended that the evidence on record indicates that the deceased was assaulted repeatedly on the head with wooden reaper which resulted in instantaneous death of deceased and this clearly indicates that the act of assault was done with an intention to cause such bodily injuries, which are likely to cause death, therefore, learned Sessions Judge is justified in holding that the act committed by the accused amounts to murder punishable under Section 302 IPC. Therefore, he sought for dismissal of the appeal.

10. In the facts and circumstances of the case and in the light of the submissions made by the learned counsel for the parties, the points that would arise for our consideration are:

10

i. Whether the learned Sessions Judge is justified in holding the death of deceased as homicidal and that the appellant / accused was responsible for the homicidal death of the deceased?
ii. Whether the learned Sessions Judge is justified in holding that the act committed by the appellant / accused amounts to murder punishable under Section 302 IPC?

11. As per the evidence on record placed by the prosecution, the incident of assault on the deceased occurred at about 9.00 p.m. on 28.04.2003, while the report of the incident was lodged by P.W.14 at about 10.45 p.m on the same day. P.W.14 in his oral evidence has stated that immediately on seeing the incident he informed the police over phone and waited for the arrival of the police. According to him when the police did not arrive at the scene of occurrence, he himself went to the police station and reported the matter to the police. This part of the evidence 11 of P.W.14 has not been seriously challenged. It is in the evidence of P.W.18 M.R.Muduvi, Police Sub Inspector, Bangarpet Police Station, that while he was discharging duty as SHO of the police station, at 10.45 p.m. on 28.04.2003, P.W.14 appeared before him and lodged oral report, which was reduced into writing as per Ex.P.6 and after reading out the contents of same, P.W.14 subscribed his signature to the same in his presence based on which he registered the case and rushed to the scene of occurrence. This part of the evidence of P.W.18 is also not seriously challenged by the defence. This shows that there was no delay in lodging the report.

12. The evidence of P.W.18 further indicates that on registration of the case, he went to the scene of occurrence, saw the dead body lying there and since it was dark he could not proceed with the investigation and thereafter, by deputing the staff for dead body watch, he went back to the head quarters and on the next day morning at 8.00 a.m. he conducted inquest over the dead body in the presence of 12 witnesses and thereafter, sent the dead body to post mortem examination. P.W.13 - Dr.Mahadevaiah, in his evidence has stated that he conducted the post mortem examination on the dead body of Narayanaswamy between 11.20 a.m. to 1.00 p.m. and noticed following injuries on the dead body.

"1) lacerated wound over left infrascapular region 1" x ½" x 2 cms; 5½ inch from midline.
2) lacerated wound 3" x ½" x ½" over occipital region 3" from left ear, ½" from midline, bone exposed at base;
3) lacerated wound on right side parietal region 3" from right ear, 1" from midline, 6" x ½" bone at base is fractured;
4) incised like lacerated wound on left frontal region 2" x ½" x ½", 21/2" from left eyebrow, ½" from midline; and,
5) lacerated wound on parietal region 2" x ½"
x ½" from midline on right side, 9" from root of nose."

13. He has further opined that the death was due to shock as a result of laceration of brain and intra-cranial haemorrhage due to head injury. This part of the evidence of P.W.13 and the contents of post mortem report have not been challenged by the defence. Thus, evidence placed on record by the prosecution establishes that the death was due 13 to intra-cranial haemorrhage. Though the defence has contended that death was due to fall and intoxication, there is no circumstance to indicate that the deceased was intoxicated nor there was any proof to indicate that the deceased fell into drain. Number of injuries sustained by the deceased over the head and his instantaneous death would clearly demolish the defence put forth by the accused. Therefore, the learned Session Judge is justified in holding that the death of deceased was homicidal.

14. According to the case of prosecution P.W.1 - Muniyappa, the brother of the accused, P.W.2, the father of the accused, PW5-Narayanappa, PW15-Munivenkatappa, cousin brother of the deceased and PW14 Chennaguruva are the eye-witnesses. However, during the trial of the case except PW14 other witnesses did not support the case of the prosecution as to the complicity of the accused. Therefore, the evidence of other witnesses are of no assistance to the prosecution. PW14-Chennaguruva in his oral evidence has supported the case of the prosecution. Reading of the 14 evidence of PW14 indicates that he was residing in the house opposite to the house of the accused and by the side of the house of the accused. According to PW14, the incident occurred at about 9.00 p.m. on 28.04.2003. According to him, at that time he was in the house and after hearing the sounds of quarrel from the side of the house of deceased Naryanaswamy he came out and there he saw both the accused and the deceased quarreling with each other verbally. He has further stated that there was street light glowing at that place and also the electrical light in the house of the deceased. According to him he saw at that time the accused picking up a wooden reaper from the heap of fire wood stored therein and assaulting the deceased on the head repeatedly. According to PW14, the accused assaulted thrice on the head of the deceased, as a result of which the deceased collapsed and had sustained severe bleeding injury on the head, and after assaulting the deceased, the accused along with the reaper went towards the high school building. He has further stated that immediately, he went near the place where Narayanaswamy was lying and found him dead. 15 The fact that PW14 is the resident of the house opposite to the house of the deceased is not disputed. The fact that the incident occurred at about 9.00 p.m. on 28.04.2003 is also not seriously challenged by the accused. Therefore, the presence of PW14 in his house at that time is quite natural, as such, he cannot be termed as a chance witness. The evidence of PW14 that on hearing the sound of galata he came out of the house is quite natural and it is consistent with the normal human conduct. The evidence of PW14 that there was sufficient light for him to identify the assailant and the overt-acts has not been challenged in the cross- examination. The evidence of PW14 is consistent with the contents of his earlier statement made in Ex.P6, since, no contradiction or inconsistency or omission is brought out in the cross-examination. No circumstance is brought out in the cross-examination to discredit the testimony of PW14. Under these circumstances, we are of the considered opinion that the learned Sessions Judge is justified in placing reliance on the testimony of PW14. It is now well settled by catena of decisions of the Apex Court that conviction can be 16 based on the testimony of a sole eye witness provided if such testimony is credit worthy and does not suffer from any inconsistency. The evidence of PW14 in our opinion is consistent, cogent and has been rightly accepted by the learned Sessions Judge to base conviction. Therefore, the finding recorded by the learned Sessions Judge that the accused was responsible for the homicidal death of deceased, is sound and reasonable as such it does not warrant interference.

15. The next question required to be considered is as to whether the act committed by the accused would amount to murder or would it be an act attracting culpable homicide not amounting to murder as contended by the learned counsel. Of course, as rightly contended by the learned counsel for the appellant, the circumstances on record does not indicate that the accused had any premeditation in causing the death of the deceased. There also appears to be no intention on the part of the accused to commit murder. Nevertheless, the intention of causing death would not be 17 the only criteria to attract the offence punishable under Section 302 of IPC. Section 300 defines murder, which sets out four circumstances under which culpable homicide would constitute murder punishable under Section 302 IPC.

According to the first circumstance the culpable homicide would be murder if the act by which the death is caused with intention of causing death, The second circumstance is if it is done with the intention of causing such bodily injury as the offender knows to be likely to cause the death of the person to whom the harm is caused.

The third circumstance is if it is done with the intention of causing bodily injury to any person and the bodily injury intended to be inflicted is sufficient in the ordinary course of nature to cause death.

16. In our considered opinion the act committed by the accused, as established from the evidence of PW14, would fall within the 2nd or 3rd circumstance of Section 300 of 18 IPC. According to the evidence of PW14 there was only a verbal quarrel between the deceased and the accused with regard to the payment of wages. It is not brought out in the evidence of PW14 that there was any kind of scuffle between the two and during the course of scuffle the blow was inflicted. It is not the case of the defence that the accused intended to inflict blow on a non-vital part and during the scuffle the blow fell on the vital part. On the other hand, the circumstance indicates that the accused after picking up a wooden reaper from the heap of fire wood, assaulted the deceased on the vital part viz., the head repeatedly resulting in four injuries. Crushing of the skull bone and pieces of the skull bone having lacerated the brain indicates the force with which the accused has dealt the blows, which resulted in instantaneous death. This repeated assault on the head with a weapon like wooden reaper would indicate that the accused had an intention to cause such bodily injury, which is likely to cause death of a person and such injury is sufficient in the ordinary course of nature to cause death of the deceased. It is reasonable to infer that the accused had 19 knowledge that if he assaults the deceased with a wooden reaper on the head repeatedly, it would result in the death. Under these circumstances, we are of the considered opinion that the act committed by the accused was culpable homicide amounting to murder punishable under Section 302 of IPC. We find absolutely no circumstance which indicate that the act committed by the accused was culpable homicide not amounting to murder falling under Section 304 of IPC. In this view of the matter, we are of the considered opinion that the learned Sessions Judge is justified in convicting the accused for the offence punishable under Section 302 of IPC. Though learned the counsel for the appellant placed reliance on several decisions, as noticed supra, in our considered opinion none of the decisions would apply to the facts of this case. It is well settled that each criminal case will have to be decided on the basis of the evidence placed on record of that case. The precedent law in criminal cases would play minimal role. The case laws relied on by the learned counsel have been decided having regard to the peculiar facts and circumstances of that case. 20 Therefore, we need not refer to those decisions in detail except saying that those decisions have no application to the facts of this case and are distinguishable on facts. In this view of the matter, we find no ground to interfere with the well reasoned judgment of the trial Court. The judgment under appeal does not suffer from any perversity or illegality warranting interference by this Court. In this view of the matter, appeal lacks merit and accordingly, the same is dismissed. The judgment of conviction and order of sentence passed by the Trial Court is hereby confirmed.
