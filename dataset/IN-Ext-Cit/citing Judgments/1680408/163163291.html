appellant and the learned counsel for the State .
2. In this appeal, the basic question that has been raised is as to whether Government can terminate the service of its illegally appointed employees, after the lapse of substantial period of service. The petitioner has challenged the validity of the order dated 16th September 2000, Annexure-1 to the writ petition issued by the District Education Officer, Banka whereby he asked the petitioner-

appellant to produce his Service Book, appointment letter, order of payment of salary and the certificates in respect of his educational qualification, in view of the report submitted by the District Education Officer, Bhagalpur to the effect that the petitioner-appellant was appointed without following the procedure of appointment and by this letter, payment of 2 salary of the petitioner-appellant was stopped. The appellant challenged the aforesaid order in the present writ petition and during the pendency of the case, the District Education Officer, Banka vide order dated 12th April 2007, terminated the services of the petitioner-appellant and the same was affirmed by the Director, Secondary Education, Govt. of Bihar by his order dated 26th March 2008. The petitioner challenged the order of termination, including the order passed by the Director, Secondary Education, vide I.A.No. 2332 of 2008.

3. It will be relevant to mention from the facts on the record that the Government vide letter dated 3rd December 1980 clearly laid down that all public appointments have to be made only after publishing the advertisement giving opportunity to all the eligible candidates and after holding due selection process, roster clearance etc. For verification of the aforesaid fact about the mode of appointment and to check his educational qualification, petitioner was issued notice vide Annexure-1 by the District Education Officer, Banka to submit relevant papers.

4. It appears from the record of the writ Court 3 that the Assistant Director, Secondary Education, vide letter no.3530 dated 24th October 1991 had directed the District Education Officer, Bhagalpur to appoint the petitioner as clerk against the vacant post, at the instance of letter of Minister, Human Resources Department, Govt. of Bihar, bearing No. 3712 dated 12th August 1991 and in pursuance thereof, the District Education Officer, vide Memo No. 7086-88 dated 1st November 1991 appointed the petitioner as clerk in the High School, Athamaha in the basic pay scale of Rs.1,200 - 1,800/- (Annexure-3). It is apparent from the aforesaid letter that the petitioner-appellant was appointed as a clerk at the behest of Minister through the Assistant Director, Secondary Education, Government of Bihar, Patna.

5. Learned counsel for the appellant has pointed out that the order passed by the District Education Officer Banka was overturned by the Regional Deputy Director of Education, Bhagalpur by his order dated 20 th April 2007 (Annexure-9 to the I.A.). The Director, Secondary Education, Bihar by order dated 26th March 2008has also held the order passed by the Regional Deputy Director, Education to be invalid and the order of the District Education Officer was restored. 4

6. According to the case of the petitioner- appellant, he joined the post and was allowed to work, but on receipt of report of the District Education Officer, Bhagalpur vide letter No.571 dated 28th May 2000, the Director, Secondary Education, vide letter No. 977 dated 11 th August 2000 asked for action to be taken against the writ petitioner and others, who were appointed illegally. In pursuance of letter No. 983 dated 15th September 2000 issued by the District Education Officer, Banka, the In-charge Head Master of the School submitted papers regarding the educational qualification of petitioner but could not produce any material to show that the petitioner was appointed to the post of clerk after following the due procedure for appointment, such as, advertisement of the post, appearing before any Selection Board and selection for appointment. The District Education Officer, Bhagalpur, vide his letter dated 27th October 2000, on consideration of his explanation and considering the material which was produced by the petitioner-appellant to the District Education Officer,Banka after due consideration and after examination of the records has come to a positive finding that the appointment of the petitioner-appellant was made illegally, as the conditions laid 5 down vide letter No. 16440 dated 3rd December 1980 were not followed and on this finding he has issued the show cause as to why the services of the petitioner be not terminated. The District Education Officer, Banka again vide letter No. 405 dated 26th March 2001 issued a letter to the appellant to file his show cause. When the District Education Officer did not receive any show cause, he again, vide letter No. 822 dated 25th July 2001 and letter no.1395 dated 17th November 2001 sent reminders.

7. On consideration of the aforesaid facts, it appears that the petitioner-appellant could not produce any material with regard to mode of his appointment as a clerk. The onus was upon the petitioner-appellant to produce the materials to show that he was validly appointed after following the due process of law. When the petitioner- appellant did not respond, ultimately the District Education Officer, Banka vide his letter No. 506 dated 12th April 2007, terminated his services on the ground of illegal appointment, as the appointment was made dehors the Rules. The order of the District Education Officer was approved by the Director, Secondary Education, Bihar, Patna vide letter No. 88 dated 26th March 2008 and the order of Regional Deputy Director 6 was overturned.

8. The District Education Officer, Banka as well as the Director, Secondary Education while passing the impugned orders have considered every aspect of the matter and have come to a finding that the appointment of the petitioner was completely illegal and violative of Articles 14 and 16 of the Constitution of India, which view has been challenged in the writ petition and the learned single Judge after due consideration, affirmed the orders.

9. The primary question that has been raised by the learned counsel for the petitioner-appellant is that the petitioner-appellant has continued in service for a long period and after lapse of such a long period, the procedure adopted for his appointment should not come under scrutiny at such a belated stage.

10. In this case, the appellant neither before the writ Court nor before us has brought any material to show that he was appointed after following the due process of appointment, such as, issuance of advertisement. calling the names of candidates from the Employment Exchange, appearance before any Selection/Interview Board, rather the facts show that he along with some other persons had been 7 appointed at the behest of Minister, Human Resources Department, Govt. of Bihar. In these circumstances, appellant‟s appointment cannot be said to be legal or irregular, rather it is out and out an illegal appointment.

11. Learned Senior counsel for the petitioner in support of his contention has relied on an unreported judgment of this Court in the case of Chandra Mauleshwar Singh Vs. The State of Bihar (C.W.J.C.No. 759 of 2009. It has been contended by the learned counsel for the petitioner that the fact and law decided by this Court completely covers the case of the petitioner-appellant and, as such, the relief granted to the aforesaid petitioner should also be granted to the present appellant.

12. He has further placed reliance on another judgment reported sin (2010)9 SCC 247 (AIR 2010 SC 2587) state of Karnataka Vs. M.L.Kesari.

13. The learned counsel for the State supported the order passed by the learned single Judge and said that State Authorities have rightly taken action, because a person appointed illegally cannot have right to continue in service and he can be terminated at any stage of service as he does not have right to hold the post. In order to examine the issue 8 at hand, it would be proper to examine cases decided by Hon‟ble Supreme Court dealing with this aspect of the matter.

14. The Supreme Court in the case of R.Vishwanatha Pillai Vs. State of Karnataka, (2004)2 SCC 105 has held that any appointment obtained by fraud and deceit is void from its inception and the beneficiary of such appointment cannot claim the protection of Article 311 of the Constitution of India. In the aforesaid case, the petitioner had obtained appointment by a false certificate and considering the same, the Supreme Court has held that since the petitioner of that case had played fraud, hence he cannot take advantage of his own fault. It will be apt to quote the following passage from the aforesaid judgment as under :

"Unless the appellant can lay a claim to the post on the basis of his appointment, he cannot claim the constitutional guarantee given under article 311 of the Constitution. As he had obtained the appointment on the basis of a false caste certificate, he cannot be considered to be a person who holds the post within the meaning of Article 311 of the Constitution of India. The position, therefore, is that 9 the appellant has usurped the post which should have gone to a member of the Scheduled Castes. In view of the finding recorded by the scrutiny committee and upheld upto this Court, he has disqualified himself to hold the post. The appointment was void from its inception. It cannot be said that the said void appointment would enable the appellant to claim that he was holding a civil post within the meaning of Article 311 of the Constitution of India. As the appellant had obtained the appointment by playing a fraud, he cannot be allowed to take advantage of his own fraud in entering the service and claim that he was holder of the post entitled to be dealt with in terms of Article 311 of the Constitution of India or the Rules framed thereunder. Where an appointment in a service has been acquired by practicing fraud or deceit, such an appointment is no appointment in law, in service and in such a situation Article 311 of the Constitution is not attracted at all.

The appellant obtained the appointment against a post meant for a reserved candidate by producing a false caste certificate and by playing a 10 fraud. His appointment to the post was void and nonest in the eye of law. A person who seeks equity must come with clean hand. He who comes to the court with false claim, cannot plead equity nor would the court be justified to exercise equity jurisdiction in his favour. A person who seeks equity must act in a fair and equitable manner. Equity jurisdiction cannot be exercised in the case of a person who got the appointment on the basis of a false caste certificate by playing a fraud. No sympathy and equitable consideration can come to his rescue. We are of the view that equity or compassion cannot be allowed to bend the arms of law in a case where an individual acquires a status by practicing fraud."

15. In a similar case, the State of Manipur Vs. V.Y.Toten Singh, (2007)5 SCC 65, the Apex Court held that any appointment made dehors Articles 14 & 16 of the Constitution is a nullity. In that case the Commissioner of Revenue made the appointment in violation of Articles 14 & 16 of the Constitution and later on the same was cancelled on the ground that the appointment was made without the knowledge of the Revenue Department of the State and no 11 records were available with the State. In that case the Supreme Court held that appointment issued in favour of persons concerned is a forged appointment and the State cannot be compelled to pay salary to them from the State Exchequer and any action which has not been taken by the authority competent therefore is complete violation of constitutional and legal frame of law which would not be binding on the State. It has further been held that discretionary power cannot be exercised in arbitrary manner and further held that if the appointment of the person concerned is nullity, the question of observance of principles of natural justice would not arise. It was the duty of the competent authority making appointment to follow the constitutional schemes as enumerated in Articles 14 and 16 of the Constitution.

16. The Supreme Court while considering the case of R.S.Garg Vs. State of Uttar Pradesh, 2006 Lab. I.C. 2996, has considered the decision in the case of State of Karnataka Vs. Uma Devi (2006)4 SCC 1 and while considering the fact that the appointment should be made following the constitutional provisions of Article 14 and 16 of the Constitution, held that and any appointment made, 12 without following the Rules of selection has to be categorized as „illegal appointment‟. It has further been held that any rule made by the Executive under Article 309 of the Constitution which is not in consonance with Articles 14 & 16 of the Constitution cannot be sustained. The Apex Court has held that appointment made in violation of mandatory provisions of the Rules for appointment has to be categorized as an illegal appointment. The Supreme Court in Para-21 of the judgment observed that the appointments dehors the rules would render the same as illegal and not irregular and has quoted passage from the judgment of Uma Devi(supra) in the following terms:

"Thus, it is clear that adherence to the rule of equality in public employment is a basic feature of our constitution and since the rule of law is the core of our Constitution, a Court would certainly be disabled from passing an order upholding a violation of Article 14 or in ordering the overlooking of the need to comply with requirements of Article 14 read with Article 16 of the Constitution. Therefore, consistent with the scheme for public employment, this Court while laying down the law, has necessarily 13 to hold that unless the appointment is in terms of the relevant rules and after a proper competition among qualified persons, the same would not confer any right on the appointee.

It has also to be clarified that merely because a temporary employee or a casual daily wage worker is continued for a time beyond the term of his appointment, he would not be entitled to be absorbed in regular service or made permanent, merely on the strength of such continuance, if the original appointment was not made by following a due process of selection as envisaged by the relevant rules. It is not open to the court to prevent regular recruitment at the instance of temporary employees whose period of employment has come to an end or of ad hoc employees who by the very nature of their appointment, do not acquire any right. High Courts acting under Article 226 of the Constitution of India, should not ordinarily issue directions for absorption, regularization, or permanent continuance unless the recruitment itself was made regularly and in terms of the constitutional scheme."

14

17. The Supreme Court in another case Ashok Kumar Sonkar Vs. Union of India, (2007)4 SCC 54 has held that if the appointment has been made without following the provisions of Articles 14 & 16 of the Constitution and the statutory rules were not followed, the appointment is illegal. In that circumstance, it has further been held that if the appointment is irregular that can be regularized but if it is illegal, it is nonest in the eye of law which renders the appointment a nullity.

18. The Supreme Court has considered the status of a person who was appointed through back-door manner and the status of illegal appointment. In this connection also the Hon‟ble Supreme Court in the case of State of Bihar Vs. Upedra Narayan Singh, 2009(4) PLJR (SC) 73 has elaborately dealt with the mode to be applied while making employment in public office. The Court has also considered the spoil system which was prevalent in the Unites States of America. While deciding this issue, the Apex Court held that in the matter of public appointment, every public employer is duty bound to notify the vacancy to the concerned Employment Exchange so as to enable it to sponsor the names of eligible candidates and also to advertise the same in 15 the News-papers having wider circulation, Employment News Bulletin, getting announcements made on Radio/ Tele- vision and consider all eligible candidates whose names may be forwarded by the concerned Employment Exchange and who may apply pursuant to the advertisement published in the News-papers or pronouncement made on Radio/Tele- vision. While dealing with the matter, the Court has held that a huge illegal employment market has developed in the country adversely affecting the legal and constitutional right of lakhs of meritorious members of younger generation of the country who are forced to seek intervention of court and wait for justice for years together. The Court has further held that the person appointed or regularized through illegal method cannot be allowed to continue in service and at any stage a Government can take appropriate step to terminate their services. In this case the Court has also dealt with a situation when the scheme is made on the basis of discretion. The Court has held that Article 14 of the Constitution is a positive concept which cannot be inferred in negative manner. When any authority is shown to have committed any illegality or irregularity in favour of any individual or group of individuals, similar illegal favourable treatment 16 cannot be granted to others. In this connection, the following paragraphs will be apt to be quoted as under:

"The illegal employment market continued to grow in the country and those entrusted with the power of making appointment and those who could pull strings in the corridor of power manipulated the system to ensure that their favorites get employment in complete and contemptuous disregard of the equality clause enshrined in Article 16 of the Constitution and Section 4 of the 1959 Act. However, the Courts gradually realized that unwarranted sympathy shown to the progenies of spoil system has eaten into the vitals of the service structure of the State and public bodies and this is the reason why relief of reinstatement and/or regularization of service has been denied to illegal appointees/back-door entrants in large number of cases."
19. A Division Bench of this Court in the case of Ashok Kumar Vs. The State of Bihar, 2009(3) PLJR 201 has considered the same situation and held that the illegal appointee has no right to continue in service, as the appointment through back-door method without following 17 the procedure of appointment is void.

20. In another case a similar view has been taken by a Division Bench of this Court in the case of State of Bihar Vs. Sheo Shankar, 2008(3) PLJR 363.

21. Learned counsel for the petitioner-appellant has stated that the appellant has continued in service for a long time and after lapse of such a long period, issue of illegality or irregularity should not be opened as the appellant was appointed against a sanctioned post and was having the requisite qualification. He has further submitted that the judgment of the Constitution Bench in the case of State of Karnataka Vs. Uma Devi, (2006)4 SCC 1 has been watered down in the State of Krnataka Vs. M.L. Kesari and it has been submitted that in this case the Court has held that if the person has been appointed against a sanctioned post having minimum requisite qualification, it will not be considered as an illegal appointment and the Hon‟ble Apex Court explained the judgment in the case of Uma Devi and did not interfere with the employment of the employee who had completed 10 years of service having requisite qualification and having been appointed against a sanctioned post.

18

22. Recently the Hon‟ble Supreme Court in another case had the occasion to consider this issue of rights of a person having been appointed through back-door method in Union of India Vs. Arulmozhi Iniarasu, 2011(4) PLJR 83(SC). The Supreme Court while considering the right of a person appointed illegally has considered the earlier judgments, except that of M.L.Kesari (supra) and has held that the person appointed through back-door method has no right to continue in service. While deciding the issue, the Supreme Court held as under:

"Para:13 Bearing in mind the aforenoted principle of law, we now refer to the decision in Nagendra Chandra. It is plain from as bare reading of the said decision that the question which fell for consideration before a Bench of three learned judges of this Court was as to whether the appointments of the appellants in that case were illegal or irregular. This Court opined that since the appointments made were not only in infraction of the recruitment rules but also violative of Article 14& 16 of the Constitution of India, these were illegal. It was thus, held that the appellants will not 19 be entitled to get the benefits of the directions contained in Umadevi case. In the said decision it has clearly been held that the courts are not expected to issue any direction for absorption/regularization or permanent continuance of temporary, contractual, casual, daily wagers or ad hoc employees merely because such an employee is continuing for a long time beyond the term of his appointment. It has also been held that such an employee would not be entitled to be absorbed in regular service or made permanent merely on the strength of such continuance, if the original appointment was not made by following a due process of selection as envisaged by the relevant rules. Therefore, in our opinion, the said observation cannot be said to be an exposition of general principle of law on the point that a long length of service de hors the relevant recruitment rules for the post, is relevant factor for the waiver or relaxation of any illegibility criterion including age limit, for future regular selection for the post.

Obviously the observation, general in nature, was 20 made by this Court in exercise of this jurisdiction under Article 142 of the Constitution of India and, therefore, cannot be treated as a binding precedent. It has to be confined to the peculiar facts of that case.

Para-23: Lastly, as regards the submission that the action of the appellant is highly discriminatory inasmuch as some similarly situated persons have been appointed/absorbed as Sepoys, the argument is stated to be rejected. It is well settled that a writ of mandamus can be issued by the High Court only when there exists a legal right in the writ petitioner and corresponding legal obligation in the State. Only because an illegality has been committed, the same cannot be directed to be perpetuated. It is trite law that there cannot be equality in illegality."

23. In view of the latest judgment of the Hon‟ble Supreme Court, the consistent view that has been taken by the Apex Court is that the appointment of a person dehors the established procedure of appointment in violation of Articles 14 & 16 of the Constitution has to be treated as „illegal appointment‟ and such appointee cannot derive the 21 right to continue in service and at any stage the Government, if it finds that the appointment has been made through illegal method, can terminate the service under a void appointment.

24. Hence, the contention raised by the writ- petitioner-appellant is rejected. The view taken by the learned single Judge is correct and in accordance with the view taken by the Hon‟ble Apex Court and hence requires no interference.

25. Accordingly, this appeal is dismissed. No costs.
