BRIEF FACTS

2. As per averments made in the plaint, plaintiff, a partnership firm, is engaged in the business of bakery i.e. manufacturing, processing, preparing and selling of breads, biscuits, cakes, rusk, pastry etc. under the its registered trademark 'SSSB' vide No.01882532. 16. Before proceeding further, reference to the case laws Suit No.52/2016 Page 11 of 24 Shri Sahu Bakers vs. Jamaluddin governing the reliefs of infringement and action of passing off seems desirable, which are reproduced as under:

(i) Ruston & Hornsby Ltd. vs. The Zamindara Engineering Co. 1969 (2) Supreme Court Cases 727, Hon'ble Supreme Court held that an infringement action is quite distinguished from a 'passing off action' although the two are similar also in same respects.

"In passing off action the issue is:­ Is the defendant selling goods so marked as to be designed or calculated to lead purchasers to believe that they are plaintiff's goods?
But in an infringement action the issue is:­ Is the defendant using a mark which is the same as or which is a coloured imitation of the plaintiff's registered trade mark.

On the other hand the gist of a passing off action is that A is not entitled to representation his goods as the goods of B but it is not necessary for B to prove that A did this knowingly or with any intent to deceive. It is enough that the get­up of B's goods has become distinctive of them and that there is a probability of confusion between them and the goods of A. No case of actual deception or any actual damage It needs to be highlighted here that be proved. At common law the action was not maintainable unles there had been fraud on A's part. In equity, however, Lord Cottenham, L. C., in Millingon v. Fox held that it was immaterial whether the defendant had been fraudulent or not in using the plaintiff's trade mark and granted an Suit No.52/2016 Page 12 of 24 Shri Sahu Bakers vs. Jamaluddin injunction accordingly. The common law courts, however, adhered to their view that fraud was necessary until the Judicature Acts, fusing law and equity, gave the suitable rule the victory over the common law rule."

(ii) Girnar Tea and Another vs. Brooke Bond (India) Ltd, Hon'ble Bombay High Court held that:

"Unlike a suit for passing off, in a suit for infringement of registered trade mark, once the plaintiff proves that similarity between his and the defendant's mark is so close, either visually, phonetically or otherwise, that there is an imitation, no further evidence is required to establish that this rights are violated.
That the two marks "Girnar" and "Gulnar" are phonetically similar is patent. It has been so found by the learned Judge and it not disputed before under Section by counsel for the Defendant.
We find, firstly that the learned Judge has not taken any account of the indubitable visual similarity of the marks "GIRNAR" and "GULNAR". He has compared the two cartons as a whoe. He could have taken other features upon the cartons into account only after concluding that the marks were visually similar and he could then, if he though they were, gone on to consider whether the added matter upon the Defendant's carton out­weighed the impression created by the visual similarity of the marks, themselves.

Secondly, if he was sure that there was no deceptive similarity between the two marks by reason of the added matter, the learned Judge would not have directed the Defendant to change the colour of its cartons and invariably to print the words "BROOKE BOND' in conjunction with the mark "GIRNAR" in the same size and Suit No.52/2016 Page 13 of 24 Shri Sahu Bakers vs. Jamaluddin colour. In the premises, there is we thing, reason to go into the matter in appeal.

The marks "GIRNAAR' and "GULNAR" are visually and phonetically very similar. It is our impression that the mark, "GULNAR" is the predominant impression conveyed by the Defendant's carton and that that impression is not out­weighed by the added matter, viz, the use of the words "GULNAR" or the depiction of the flower beneath it. We are, therefore, of the view that the two marks are deceptively similar."

(iii) Kaviraj Pandit Durga Dutt Sharma vs. Navaratna Pharmaceutical Laboratories (1965) 1 SCR 737: AIR 1965 SC 980, Hon'ble Supreme Court observed:

"29. When once the use by the defendant of the mark which is claimed to infringe the plaintiff's mark is shown to be "in the course of trade", the question whether there has been an infringement is to be decided by comparison of the two marks, Where the two marks are identical no further questions arise; for then the infringement is made out.

When the two marks are not identical, the plaintiff would have to establish that the mark used by the defendant so nearly resembles the plaintiff's registered trade­dress as is likely to deceive or cause confusion and in relation to goods in respect of which it is registered (Vide Section 21). A point has sometimes been raised as to whether the words "or cause confusion" introduce any element which is not already covered by the words "likely to deceive" and it has sometimes been answered by saying that it is merely an extension of the earlier test and does not add very materially to the concept indicated by the earlier words "likely to deceive". But this apart, as the question arises in an action for infringement the onus would be on the plaintiff to establish that the trademark used by the Suit No.52/2016 Page 14 of 24 Shri Sahu Bakers vs. Jamaluddin defendant in the course of trade in the goods in respect of which his mark is registered, is deceptively similar. This has necessarily to be ascertained by a comparison of the two marks­ the degree of resemblance which is necessary to exist to cause deception not being capable of definition by laying down objective standards. The persons who would be deceived are, of course, the purchasers of the goods and it is the likelihood of their being deceived that is the subject of consideration. The resemblance may be phonetic, visual or in the basic idea represented by the plaintiff's mark. The purpose of the comparison is for determining whether the essential features of the plaintiff's trademark are to be found in that used by the defendant. The identification of the essential features of the mark is in essence a question of fact and depends on the judgment of the Court based on the evidence led before it has regards the usage of the trade. It should, however, be borne in mind that the object of the equity in ultimate analysis is whether the mark used by the defendant as a whole is deceptively similar to that of the registered mark of the plaintiff."

(iv) In, N. R. Dongre vs. Whirlpool Corporation 1996 (16) PTC 583 (SC), Hon'ble Supreme Court held that:

"An infringement action is available where there is violation of specific property right acquired under and recognized by the statute. In a passing off action, however, the plaintiff's right is independent of such a statutory right to a trademark and is against the conduct of the defendant which lead to or is intended or calculated to lead to deception. Passing off is said to be a species of unfair trade competition or of actionable unfair trading by which one person through deception, attempts to obtain an economic benefit of the reputation which another has established for himself in a particular trade or business. The action is regarded as an action for deceit. The tort of passing off Suit No.52/2016 Page 15 of 24 Shri Sahu Bakers vs. Jamaluddin involves a misrepresentation made by a trader to his prospective customers calculated to injure, as a reasonably foreseeable consequence, the business or goodwill of another which actually or probably, causes damages to the business or goo of the other trader."

(v) In, Wander Ltd and Another vs. Antox India P ltd. 1990 Supp Supreme Court Cases 727, Hon'ble Supreme Court held that:

"An infringement action is available where there is violation of specific property right acquired under and recognized by the statute. In a passing off action, however, the plaintiff's right is independent of such a statutory right to a trademark and is against the conduct of the defendant which lead to or is intended or calculated to lead to deception. Passing off is said to be a species of unfair trade competition or of actionable unfair trading by which one person through deception, attempts to obtain an economic benefit of the reputation which another has established for himself in a particular trade or business. The action is regarded as an action for deceit. The tort of passing off involves a misrepresentation made by a trader to his prospective customers calculated to injure, as a reasonably foreseeable consequence, the business or goodwill of another which actually or probably, causes damages to the business or goo of the other trader. Speaking of the legal clarification of this form of action, Lord Diplock said:
" Unfair trading as a wrong actionable at the suit of other traders who thereby suffer loss of business or goodwill may take a variety of forms to some of which separate labels have become attached in English law.
Conspiracy to injure a person in his trade or business is one, slander of goods another, but most protean is that which is generally and nowadays, perhaps Suit No.52/2016 Page 16 of 24 Shri Sahu Bakers vs. Jamaluddin misleading, described as "passing­off". The form that unfair trading takes will alter with the ways in which trade is carried on and business reputation and goodwill acquired. Emerson's maker of the better mousetrap if secluded in his house built in the woods would today be unlikely to find a path beaten to his door in the absence of a costly advertising campaign to acquaint the public with the excellence of his wares"

(vi) In, Sarabhai International Ltd & Another vs. Sara Exports International AIR 1988 Delhi 134, Hon'ble Delhi High Court held that:

"The aforesaid articles bearing the trade mark SARA and SARABHAI denoted and connoted the goods of the plaintiff and no one else, and was an indication of the source of manufacture as such. That there were common fields of activities in respect of the similar goods of plaintiffs and defendant. The adoption of trading style Sara Export International by the defendant and offering for sale, export and advertising the goods under the aforesaid trading style, that is Sara Exports International was calculated to deceive and create confusion amongst the doctors, chemists and the general public in India and the Importers and general public abroad. The use of the trading style Sara Exports International created an impression in the mind of the general public, dealers and importers that defendant may be an organization or a Division or Branch of the plaintiffs."

(vii) In, Baker Hughes Limited vs. Hiroo Khushaiani 1998 PTC (18), Hon'ble High Court held that:

18. These issues are taken up first as they are Suit No.52/2016 Page 18 of 24 Shri Sahu Bakers vs. Jamaluddin interconnected. Having regard to the above submission, and having gone through the records placed by the plaintiff, the Court is of the opinion that the defendant, in utter disregard to the Intellectual Property rights of the plaintiff, has copied the trademark, the colour scheme, the trade­dress of the plaintiff, indiscriminately, and to such an extent that even the spelling mistakes have been copied, without any application of mind. The similarity between the trademark and trade­dress/ packaging is to such an extent that the plaintiff's case "likelihood of confusion" is fully established. True that in the trademark adopted by the defendant, there is difference of letters "S" in as much as the plaintiff's trademark comprises of "SSSB" and the defendant's mark is "SB", which is super imposed over the graphic representation of Sun with rays emanating therefrom, but the difference is just symbolic, and carefully crafted.

19. The test of confusion is to be applied with a view point of an ordinary customer, and not necessarily from the view point of informed customer. Having regard to the fact that the products are common, merchandise, being used by one and all, and the customer base which also includes a section of people who may not be able to notice the changes, between the two marks, there is increased Suit No.52/2016 Page 19 of 24 Shri Sahu Bakers vs. Jamaluddin likelihood of confusion.

20. As regards the deception, the overall get up, as has been used by the plaintiff for its trademark and packaging, has been copied indiscriminately and there is no evidence on record that the same is bonafide. By any stretch of imagination such an imitation cannot be assumed to be bonafide or innocent adoption of the trademark and trade­ dress of the plaintiff. Besides since the plaintiff was able to highlight certain similarities, it fell upon the defendant, quite heavily, to prove the adoption as bonafide and not to deceive.

21. The mark adopted by the defendant, when collated with that of the plaintiff, shows that the defendant had willfully and consciously adopted the term "SB", sign of "Sun with emanating rays", positioning of other texts/ symbols which is strikingly similar to that of the plaintiff. Though, there are certain modifications which may be claimed as not too insignificant, but the same are not too persuasive and strong enough, either visually or phonetically, to obviate confusion, and as such plaintiff has been able to prove that he has actionable case against the defendant.

22. Plaintiff's mark was registered subsequently to the filing of the suit. Even if the case of the plaintiff is treated Suit No.52/2016 Page 20 of 24 Shri Sahu Bakers vs. Jamaluddin to be not maintainable in view of the non­registration as on the date of filing of the suit, the fact remains that the plaintiff's case for passing off would still be maintainable.

23. Having regard to the evidence placed, the failure of the defendant to proved that the adoption of the trademark and trade­dress / packaging is bonafide or innocent one, the plaintiff has proved his case for passing off, and as such is entitled to protection and other reliefs provided in Section 134 and 135 of the Trademark Act, 1999. The issues are accordingly decided in favour of the plaintiff.
