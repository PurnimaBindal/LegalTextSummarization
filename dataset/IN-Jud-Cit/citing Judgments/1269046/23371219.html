                         JUDGMENT

This appeal is filed challenging the judgment of conviction and sentence dated 13.10.2010 passed in Special Case No.130/2004, on the file of the Principal Sessions Judge, Bangalore Rural District, Bangalore, for the offence punishable under Section 13(1)(e) read with Section 13(2) of the Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 ('the Act' for short).

2. The factual matrix of the case is that the accused had joined the service as a Junior Engineer in Karnataka State Constructions Corporation, Bengaluru on 22.06.1979 and he was promoted as a resident Engineer in 1992. The accused had worked in Bengaluru, Tumkuru and Mysuru. The cheque period is taken as from 22.06.1979 to 28.10.1995. The accused retired from service in May 2004 and the charge-sheet came to filed in May 2004.

7. The accused was secured and he denied the charges levelled against him. The prosecution in order to prove the case, examined nine witnesses and relied upon the documents at Exs.P.1 to 27. The defence also got marked Ex.D.1 and Ex.C.1. The Trial Court after considering both oral and documentary evidence placed on record, convicted the accused in coming to the conclusion that the accused has committed an offence punishable under Section 13(1)(e) read with Section 13(2) of the 6 Act and sentenced the accused to undergo rigorous imprisonment for a period of two years and to pay fine of Rs.18,00,000/- and in default of payment of fine, rigorous imprisonment for a period of six months. Hence, the present appeal is filed before this Court.

8. The main contention in this appeal is that the prosecution has failed to establish that the accused was required to file his assets and liabilities every year. The Corporation has not taken any action for having not filed the annual property returns as per the Karnataka Civil Services (Conduct Rules). Rule 23 is applicable to the Government Servants as per the Conduct Rules. The Government Servant means any person appointed to any civil service or post in connection with the affairs of the State of Karnataka. The accused was working in the Corporation, which is a public undertaking. It is also contended that the Corporation by notification dated 20.08.1993 directed the employees to furnish the assets and liabilities statement on an annual basis as is done by the civil servants of the State. The same was challenged by the employees before the Assistant Labour Officer and stay was granted and the same was challenged before this Court and this Court did not interfere with 7 the order of the Assistant Labour Officer in terms of Ex.C.1. The prosecution has not made out how the provisions of KCSR is applicable to the appellant. The finding of the Trial Court that KCSR is applicable is not on the basis of any records. The Trial Judge has committed an error that Government notification dated 29.10.1981 is applicable. The said notification is not applicable to the accused, as he is not a Government servant. The subsequent notification dated 20.08.1993 was stayed and hence he was not required to submit the same. The Trial Judge has come to an erroneous conclusion that the accused is not a workman. The observation that said fact cannot be considered and all the income which were not disclosed to the Department has to be considered as disproportionate to the known source of income. The said finding suffers from non-application of mind.

9. The learned counsel would also submit that the witness who was examined before the Court has specifically admitted that Conduct Rules are not applicable to them and the Circular was stayed. When such being the case, the Special Judge ought not to have come to the conclusion that the Conduct Rules are applicable to the accused and he was bound to submit the assets and liabilities statement. The very approach 8 of the Trial Court with regard to the filing of the assets and liabilities is erroneous. The learned counsel would also submit that in the source report itself, it is specifically mentioned that the employees of the said Corporation were not filing the assets and liabilities. When such being the case, the Trial Court has committed an error.

10. The other contention is that the accused was asked to submit Schedule 1 to 10 and accordingly he has submitted the details supporting the documents. The prosecution has not examined the person, who registered the case and conducted the raid. Hence, it is clear that the source furnished by the accused is correct and there was no scope for filing the charge-sheet and this aspect was ignored by the Trial Judge.

Having discussed with regard to the material available on record and also the defense available before the Court, the accused has made out the probable case for acquitting the accused and the very approach of the Trial Court is erroneous and not considered the material available on record in toto, particularly, the admission of PWs.7 and 9 regarding amassing of wealth disproportionate to known sources of income and when these admissions are considered with proper perspective, the trial Judge ought not to have convicted the accused. Instead these are the admissions are not discussed in the judgment of the Trial Court and instead of it appears carried away with the case of the prosecution and erroneously come to the conclusion that the accused has amassed the wealth to the tune of 74%. It is important to note that while taking the permission to conduct the raid, it is the case of the prosecution that the disproportionate income is 240% and while filing the charge sheet it was 310% and after the trial, the trial Judge come to the conclusion that it was 74% and failed to take note 65 of the admission in respect of the worth of gold and jewels and also the value of the house property, which belongs to the accused - wife and there is a categorical admission that father- in-law had constructed the said house. When the categorical admission available, the trial Judge ought not to have proceeded to convict the accused. Hence, the Judgment of the trial Court is perverse and not based on the material available on record and also the admissions elicited from the mouth of the witnesses, particularly, PWs.4, 7 and 9. The admission given by PWs.4, 7 and 9 takes away the case of prosecution and the same is fatal to the case of the prosecution. When such being the case, the Trial Judge ought not to have convicted the accused. Hence, it is a fit case to reverse the findings of the Trial Court in coming to the conclusion that the prosecution has failed to prove the case against the accused.
