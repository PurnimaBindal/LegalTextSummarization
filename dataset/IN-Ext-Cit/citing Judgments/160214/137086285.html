                               ORAL JUDGMENT

The present petition is filed under Articles 14 & 226 of the Constitution of India as well as under the Gujarat Town Planning & Urban Development Act, 1976 for the prayers, inter alia, that the action of the respondent authorities continuing the reservation of the land owned by the petitioner bearing Survey Nos. 582/1 & 582/2 situated at Vadodara may be quashed and set aside as unreasonable, arbitrary and violative of Art. 14 & 19 of the Constitution of India and for a declaration that the reservation has lapsed. As a corollary, it has also prayed for quashing and setting aside the Notifications dated 18.1.2012 and 24.9.2012 on the grounds stated in the memo of petition as well as the amendment.

2. Heard learned advocate Shri Navin Pahwa appearing for M/s. Thakkar Associates for the petitioner, learned AGP Shri Bharat Vyas for respondent No.1 and learned advocate Shri Maulik Nanavati for respondent No. 3- Vadodara Municipal Corporation.

3. Learned counsel Shri Pahwa for the petitioner referred to the background of facts and submitted that the land in question was reserved by the respondents since 1996. Thereafter, a representation has been also made in 2006 and the petitioner had given the notice as required under sec. 20 of the Gujarat Town Planning & Urban Development Act, 1976 (hereinafter referred to as 'the Act'). Learned counsel Shri Pahwa referred to sec. 20 of the Act which reads as under :

"Sec. 20. Acquisition of land (1) The area development authority or any other authority for whose purpose land is designated in the final development plan for any purpose specified in clause (b), clause (d), [clause (f)], clause (k), clause (n) [or clause(o)] of sub- section (2) of section 12, may acquire the land either by agreement or under the provisions of the Land Acquisition Act, 1894.

Page 2 of 14

C/SCA/3992/2013 JUDGMENT (2) If the land referred to in sub-section (1) is not acquired by agreement within a period of ten years from the date of the coming into force of the final development plan or if proceedings under the Land Acquisition Act, 1894 I of 1894, are not commenced within such period, the owner or any person interested in the land may serve a notice on the authority concerned requiring it to acquire the land and if within six months from the date of service of such notice the land is not required or no steps are commenced for its acquisition, the designation of the land as aforesaid shall be deemed to have lapsed."

4. Learned counsel Shri Pahwa, therefore, submitted that as provided in this provision, if, after the land is reserved, it is not acquired for the public purpose for which it is reserved, then the owner can issue a notice as required under sec. 20(2) of the Act. He submitted that in the facts of the case, the notices have been given by the petitioner on 4.8.1980 and 21.9.2011. However, as the respondents have failed to take steps for acquisition of the land in question even after the notice under sec. 20(2) of the Act was issued, it is required to be released. He submitted that as per the scheme of the Act, after the Town Planning Scheme, within a period of 10 years the land has to be acquired which has been reserved and if it is not acquired, then the owner can give notice. He submitted that even after the notice if the acquisition proceedings are not initiated within six months, then the reservation would lapse. He therefore submitted that the decision of the respondent authorities to keep the land under reservation without actual acquisition for such a long period is illegal. He submitted that the petitioner has a right to the property which may not be a fundamental right, but still when such right of the petitioner regarding enjoyment is sought to be curtailed, the provision of the statute has to be construed strictly.

5. For that purpose, he referred to Annexure-O which is the Resolution and submitted that even their own resolution of the Board of Vadodara Urban Development Authority (VUDA) acknowledges the fact about the notice. Learned counsel Shri Pahwa pointedly referred to this to support his submissions with regard to the reservation. He submitted that on failure of Page 3 of 14 C/SCA/3992/2013 JUDGMENT VUDA, respondent No. 2, to initiate the proceedings for acquisition, the provisions of sec. 20(2) of the Act would be attracted. He submitted that, in fact, on earlier occasion the petitioner had raised an objection and had also suggested that part of the land may be released suggesting change of zone. He submitted that in fact the resolution refers to the fact that for institutional use for Vadodara Municipal Corporation (VMC) the parking zone could be converted into residential zone. He therefore submitted that the present petition may be allowed.

6. An affidavit-in-reply has been filed by respondent No.2 VUDA contending that the land in question were reserved for Local Centre by VMC. It is contended that the Second Revised Draft Development Plan of VUDA was prepared and published on 23.11.2006 and as per the requirement of VMC it was thought fit to keep the land for institutional use by VMC and Parking Zone under sec. 12(2)(0) of the Act. The Second Revised Draft Development Plan has been notified on 18.1.2012 which has come into effect and therefore the land in question is kept for institutional use by VMC and also for the Parking Zone. It is also contended that earlier the original owner of the property had approached the High Court of Gujarat by way of Special Civil Application No. 16159 of 2011 challenging the reservation and the same was disposed of by the High Court vide order dated 21.10.2011 permitting the petitioners to make a representation. It is contended that the representation has been considered by respondents Nos. 1 & 2 and the resolution has been passed that the representation is not accepted by respondent No. 1 considering the requirement of the land in question for public purpose.

7. The affidavit-in-reply is also filed by respondent No. 1 referring to the background of facts and the earlier petition being Special Civil Application No. 16159 of 2011 which was filed by the original petitioner which came to be disposed of as withdrawn with a view to permit the original owner to make a representation.

8. An affidavit-in-reply is also filed by respondent No. 3 contending, inter alia, that the petitioner has purchased the land from the erstwhile owner by registered sale deed dated 7.2.2012 and before such purchase the land in question was already reserved for public institution for VMC and parking zone. It is therefore contended that the challenge to the notification dated 18.1.2012 by which the Government of Gujarat revised the development plan suffers from the vice of delay and laches. It is contended that the revised development plan was published by the Area Development Authority in terms of sec. 13 of the Act of 1976 on 24.11.2006. It is contended that the fact of reservation of the land was known to the original owner in 2006 and in fact objections were also made by the erstwhile owner. Thereafter the procedure has been followed inviting objections with respect to the modification and thereafter Special Civil Application No. 16159 of 2011 was filed by the original owner before the High Court for the same prayer raising the same issues and ultimately it was withdrawn with a view to make representation.

9. It is therefore contended that the petitioner was fully aware of the fact that the land was under reservation prior to the date of purchase and they have not chosen to challenge such reservation immediately or within a reasonable time and therefore the claim is bad for the delay, laches and acquiescence. It is therefore contended that when the original owner of the land in earlier round of litigation has forgone the right to challenge the reservation and has sold the land without any reference to the reservation, the new purchaser who will be stepping in his shoes cannot challenge or agitate the same issue. It is contended that the petitioner who has acquired interest in the land after publication of the notification reserving the land for public purpose could not contest such reservation. It is also contended that the Corporation has initiated steps towards acquisition of the land reserved for public purpose by issuing notice to the owners of the land. It is contended that the notice dated 4.2.2013 was issued by the Corporation to the owners of the land bearing Survey No. 582/2 Page 5 of 14 C/SCA/3992/2013 JUDGMENT informing about the reservation calling upon them for discussion and therefore it has been contended that the petition is misconceived.

10. It is therefore contended that the petitioner is now estopped from challenging the reservation of the land and the petition deserves to be dismissed. It is also contended that the ground in the petition that despite service of notice upon the authority for whom the land has been reserved, it has not acquired the land or taken steps in that direction is factually incorrect. It is contended that the land bearing Survey No. 582/2 came to be reserved for parking zone and institutional area in January 2012 on publication of the notification by the State Government. It is contended that final development plan reserving the land in question for parking zone and institutional purpose for VMC is notified in January 2012 and therefore the period has not expired.

11. Learned counsel Shri Maulik Nanavati appearing for respondent No.3 has referred to the background of facts that the petitioner is the subsequent purchaser of the land in question. He submitted that the original owner had raised an objection and in fact the present petitioner who has purchased the land cannot stand on a better footing than the original owner. Learned counsel Shri Nanavati submitted that the petitioner has to step in the shoes of the original owner who had not only raised an objection under the Act which has been considered but had also challenged by way of Special Civil Application No. 16159 of 2011 before the High Court. He emphasised that the said petition was withdrawn with a view to make a representation. Learned counsel Shri Nanavati submitted that the representation has been made which has been considered and turned down. He submitted that having raised the same issue before the court and withdrawn with a view to make a representation, it cannot be permitted to agitate the same issue over again and again. He submitted that the present petitioner having purchased the land from the original owner, will step in their shoes and will also have to address with regard to the knowledge or otherwise about such proceedings including reservation. He submitted that Page 6 of 14 C/SCA/3992/2013 JUDGMENT the case of the petitioner also suffers from delay, laches and acquiescence for which he referred to the papers including the synopsis of the dates of events and submitted that for the first time he has challenged or raised this issue in 2012. After the earlier petition filed by the original owner and the representation made by him have been disposed of, a fresh round of litigation is sought to be created by the petitioner, who is a subsequent purchaser. He therefore submitted that whether the petitioner who is a subsequent purchaser had raised such issues or whether he would be bound on the ground of estoppel from raising and agitating the same issue will have to be considered.

12. Learned counsel Shri Nanavati referred to the provisions of sec. 20 of the Act and emphasised the words 'authority concerned'..... He submitted that the language of the section clearly provides that the notice has to be served to the authority concerned, meaning thereby, the authority for which the reservation has been made, that the reservation is made for VMC and no notice has been served to VMC. He submitted that therefore the entire bogey raised referring to the lapse of reservation after a particular period and the notice having been served is misconceived in factual matrix. He submitted that the same public purpose still remains valid and in fact the petitioner or its predecessor having made an attempt for change of zone for part of the land and accepting the reservation clearly suggests that it was accepted that the land in question is reserved for public purpose. He submitted that in fact the notice as required under the law has not been given to the 'authority concerned'. Learned counsel Shri Nanavati submitted that in any case the period has to be counted from the date of publication of the final scheme in the year 2012 and the notices have been issued to the owners and the steps for acquisition have been taken. He therefore submitted that all the contentions raised are required to be considered in background of the facts particularly when the petitioner has for the first time raised such an issue in the year 2012 and therefore he cannot raise the contentions referring to sec. 20 of the Act.

13. Learned counsel Shri Nanavati referred to Annexure-O (Resolution) and submitted that there is a reference to the representation of the petitioner or his predecessor-in-title but no notice as required under sec. 20(2) is issued. He submitted that it is required to be examined whether Annexure-O can be said to be a notice as contemplated under sec. 20(2) of the Act. He referred to the papers and submitted that either the objection as per the Act has been made by the original owner or subsequently the representation has been made by the original owner and the notice as required under sec. 20 has not been given. He submitted that in the objections of 1980 made by the original owner, there is a reference with regard to the lapse of reservation. However, the petitioner who is a subsequent purchaser has raised the issue in 2012 and therefore in light of the withdrawal of the earlier petition, he is estopped from raising such contention on the ground of estoppel as well as the principle analogous to O.2 R.2.

14. Learned counsel Shri Nanavati referred to O.2 R.2 and submitted that the basic idea is that the party who is filing the proceedings is deemed to have asked for the same or all the reliefs and having failed the petition is withdrawn to make a representation. He submitted that now it is not open for the petitioner to re-agitate the same issue. He submitted that though O.2 R.2 refers to proceedings between the same party but in the proceedings by mere change of name of the party it will not make any difference and what the court has to look for is the substance. He emphasised and submitted that, can it be said that the present petitioner was not aware about such steps taken by the original owner, and therefore would it not fall in line having stepped in the shoes? He therefore submitted that it would be deemed to have taken all the steps which the original owner has taken for all purposes and the petitioner cannot approbate and reprobate that for the purpose suitable to it the original owner is deemed to have taken the necessary steps but for such other steps it may not be binding it. He submitted that in fact the alternate suggestion for the change of zone for part of the land itself suggests that the original owner had accepted the Page 8 of 14 C/SCA/3992/2013 JUDGMENT reservation.

15. Learned counsel Shri Nanavati referred to the Notification dated 18.1.2012 at Annexure-G. He has also referred to the judgment of the High Court in the case of Babulal Badriprasad Varma v. Surat Municipal Corporation and ors., reported in 2008 (3) GLH 137. Again, learned counsel Shri Nanavati has referred to the papers and submitted that only after the notification was issued in 2012, the present petitioner has made the representation but it cannot be said that it was a notice and till then there is no such notice under sec. 20(2) of the Act even by the earlier owner. He submitted that the synopsis of the facts would clearly point out that till then there is no whisper about such notice. Learned counsel Shri Nanavati submitted that assuming without admitting that what has been addressed is considered to be a notice as required under sec. 20(2) of the Act, still, considering the public interest, the present petition may not be entertained in exercise of the discretionary jurisdiction under Art. 226 of the Constitution of India. He submitted that there is no fundamental right and at the most it is a legal right. Therefore, the discretion under Art. 226 may not be exercised considering the public interest as, otherwise, it would frustrate the object of the Act and also the public interest.

16. In view of these rival submissions, it is required to be considered whether the present petition can be entertained and allowed.

17. As it transpires from the background of facts referred to hereinabove, the land in question was reserved for the Local Centre by VMC. When the revised draft plan of VUDA was prepared and published on 23.11.2006 it was kept for institutional use by the VMC and parking zone. The emphasis by learned counsel Shri Pahwa referring to sec. 20(2) of the Act that as provided in sec. 20(2) if the land is not acquired, the owner or the person interested in the land Page 9 of 14 C/SCA/3992/2013 JUDGMENT may serve notice to the authority concerned requiring it to acquire the land. If within six months from the date of service the land is not acquired or steps are not taken for acquisition, the designation of the land shall be deemed to have lapsed. Therefore, the underlying purpose of sec. 20(2) of the Act is that the land should be acquired by the authority or the government for the purpose for which it is reserved and if necessary steps are not taken for a period of 10 years, then, the owner or the person interested in the land may serve the notice calling upon the government or the authority concerned to take steps for acquisition, failing which the land would be designated or de-reserved.

18. Thus, it provides for some amount of obligation that the land could be acquired for public purpose, but there has to be a duration or limitation. However, the fact remains that for various reasons the development plan is not finalized and it is delayed during the process itself. In fact, the period of 10 years, therefore, has been provided. Again, it is required to be emphasized that the period of 10 years has a reference to the final development plan and not the draft development plan, meaning thereby, the revised or the draft development plan is required to be finalized making the picture clear as regards the purpose for which the land is reserved for public purpose. Therefore, the submission made by learned counsel Shri Pahwa referring to sec. 20(2) of the Act is misconceived on the ground that it has a reference to final development plan and not the revised development plan and it has been approved in January 2012. (emphasis supplied)

19. Again, the submissions which have been made referring to the papers clearly suggest that the original owner had made a representation to the authority. In fact, when the earlier petition being Special Civil Application No. 16159 of 2011 was preferred, the same was disposed of vide order dated 21.10.2011 permitting the petitioner to prefer the representation before the competent authority. This itself would suggest that the original owner having filed the petition earlier has withdrawn the same with a view to make a Page 10 of 14 C/SCA/3992/2013 JUDGMENT representation and had not raised any such issues relying upon sec. 20(2) of the Act. However, the subsequent purchaser now claims to raise such an issue and the present petition is filed in the name of the subsequent purchaser, though the issue would remain the same. It is in this background the submissions are required to be appreciated in context of the aspect, whether there was a representation or a notice as required under sec. 20(2) of the Act. As stated above, there where representions made and no notice as required under sec. 20(2) of the Act was given by the original owner. Again, the language employed in sec. 20(2) of the Act clearly provides that the notice is required to be served on the "authority concerned", meaning thereby, the notice ought to have been served to the respondent VMC. As no such notice calling upon the respondent VMC to de-reserve the land on failing to acquire the land has been served, such contentions are misconceived. (emphasis supplied)

20. It is a well-accepted rule of interpretation that the statutory provision has to be read as it is and if the meaning is clear and unambiguous, no external aid is necessary. In the facts of the case, the legislature has specifically used the word "authority concerned" implying that the authority for which the land is kept for reservation is the "authority concerned". In the facts of the case, VMC is the authority concerned to whom such notice under sec. 20(2) of the Act should have been served, which is not served.

21. Again, considering the sequence of events and the background as well as the fact that earlier petition being Special Civil Application No. 16159 of 2011 was withdrawn with a view to make representation clearly suggests that the original owner having failed in getting any relief, has withdrawn the petition only to make a representation. Further, merely because this petition is filed by the subsequent purchaser, the provision of O.2 R.2 may not be strictly attracted, but still the principles of issue estoppel will always be applicable. As rightly contended, the present petitioner would step in the shoes of the original owner and therefore it has to fall in line while making any such grievance. Therefore, Page 11 of 14 C/SCA/3992/2013 JUDGMENT the present petitioner cannot be heard to say raising such issues which could have been raised at the time of the earlier petition which was withdrawn. Therefore, the issues which were raised or which could have been raised now cannot be permitted to be raised merely by filing a subsequent petition in the name of other person. It is required to be mentioned that sec. 20(2) clearly provides that the owner or the person interested may give a notice and therefore it was very much within the knowledge of the present petitioner to take appropriate steps. Therefore, now it cannot be said that he was not aware, though the earlier petition was withdrawn by the original owner.

22. The reliance placed by learned counsel Shri Pahwa on the judgment in the case of V.D. Barot v. State of Gujarat and ors., reported in (2002) 10 SCC 668, is in a given set of facts suggesting that hypertechnical view should be avoided and the second petition would be maintainable. However, assuming that the petition is maintainable, it is required to be examined depending on the facts and background with reference to the issue estoppel.

23. A useful reference can be made to the observations made by the Hon'ble Apex Court in a judgment in the case of Babulal Badriprasad Varma v. Surat Municipal Corporation and ors., (supra), on this very issue regarding notice where the Hon'ble Apex Court has considered that the earlier conduct would disentitle the person from claiming an equitable relief and the conduct would preclude the person and operate as an estoppel against him. The underlying object of the Act has to be seen that with the development as provided in the final development plan every person is benefited and the owner or the person interested is required to make appropriate claim before the appropriate authority at an appropriate stage. The submissions made by learned counsel Shri Pahwa referring to Art. 14 or the Right to Property are therefore misconceived.

24. The reliance placed on the judgment of the Hon'ble Apex Court reported Page 12 of 14 C/SCA/3992/2013 JUDGMENT in (2003) 2 SCC 111 in the case of Bhavnagar University v. Palitana Sugar Mill (P) Ltd. and ors., has to be read in the background and context of the facts. However, in this judgment, for the purpose of 10 years period for the operation of sec. 20(2), specifically it has been made clear that the draft development plan which has been sanctioned and notified shall be the final development plan and the period of 10 years has to be calculated from the date of notification when such final development plan came into force.

25. A useful reference can also be made to the judgment of this High Court in the case of Deepakbhai J. Shah v. State of Gujarat, reported in 2004 (3) GLH 789, wherein referring to this very issue regarding notice under sec. 20(2) of the Act observations have been made. There also the reference is made to the 'authority concerned' and it has been observed that the notice under sec.20(2) was required to be served upon the authority concerned, i.e., the Gujarat Slum Clearance Board and the notice served upon VUDA would not be sufficient.

26. In any view of the matter, having regard to the facts and circumstances as discussed above and the rival claims, the Court, while exercising discretion under Art. 226 of the Constitution of India, is required to strike a balance between the public interest and the right of the citizen. As stated above, the petitioner as the subsequent purchaser or the person interested could have a claim as provided in the statute. However, when the original owner having failed, and when the subsequent purchaser is again raising the issues, he would be estopped from raising such contentions. The court is required to consider and strike a balance between public interest and private interest.

27. Therefore, the present petition deserves to be dismissed and accordingly stands dismissed. Rule is discharged.
