JUDGMENT

SUIT UNDER SECTIONS 105 & 106 OF THE TRADE AND MERCHANDISE MARKS ACT 1958 AS WELL AS UNDER SECTION 51 OF INDIAN COPYRIGHT ACT, 1957 FOR INFRINGEMENT AND PASSING OFF AND FOR THE RELIEF OF PERMANENT INJUNCTION AND RENDITION OF ACCOUNTS ETC.

BRIEF FACTS AND REASONS FOR DECISION :-

1. The present suit was filed by the plaintiffs for infringement and passing off and for the relief of permanent injunction and rendition of accounts etc. It is submitted by the plaintiff that it is engaged in the business of manufacturing and marketing medicinal preparations and substances for eye ailments. Plaintiff is proprietor of trademark of UJALA logo which is used by the plaintiff as distinctive mark, get up, make up, composition, illustration, manner of writing which is new and original in character. Plaintiff has copyright in the said artwork. Plaintiff has also filed application no. 711813 dated July 1996 for registration of trademark in respect of ayurvedic medicines falling in Class 5 of Fourth Schedule of Trade and Merchandise Act, 1958. Plaintiff is prior user of the trademark and its logo and continuous user thereafter. License to manufacture and market the goods was obtained by plaintiff under Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940. The user of plaintiff is long, continuous, extensive and exclusive both in domestic and international market. Plaintiff has acquired goodwill and enviable reputation and running business in lakhs of rupees every year. Plaintiff has advertised its goods through various media which includes magazines, trade literature, display boards, trade in novelties and calenders.

2. The defendant is engaged in business of manufacturing and marketing medicinal and Unani preparations to prevent and cure of cataract and other eye ailments who has adopted trademark UJALA logo in relation to his products which has striking resemblance to the trademark of the plaintiff. Defendant has adopted it to pass goods of defendant and to encash upon goodwill and reputation of the plaintiff and to make unlawful gain by passing off spurious goods. The purchaser will get impression that the goods belong to the plaintiff and it will create confusion in the mind of purchaser. The act of defendant is only dishonest, fraudulent and improper. The defendant has not given up use of the trademark and its logo despite requests by plaintiff and common friends.

3. Accordingly, plaintiff has prayed for permanent injunction against the defendant and his agents from using the deceptively similar mark to the trademark of the plaintiff. Permanent injunction is also prayed against the defendants and their agents from manufacturing, selling, offering for sale, advertising, directly or indirectly dealing with medicinal and Unani preparations to prevent and cure of cataract and other eye ailments or any allied goods under the trademark UJALA logo or doing any other thing likely to cause confusion and deception and passing off goods by defendant as goods of plaintiff. It is further prayed that defendants be directed to deliver the infringed packaging, labels, blocks, dice, articles etc. to the plaintiff for destruction or their erasure. It is further prayed that defendants be directed for rendition of accounts of profits illegally earned or in the alternative a decree towards damages alongwith the cost of the suit.

4. Defendant has submitted that defendant is registered proprietor of trademark UJALA vide no. 730634 and 1356947 in Class 5 in respect of Unani medicine for eye ailment, Unani eye drops since the year 1974. The defendant is registered copyright holder of the artistic work in the label mark UJALA as well as in the literature contained therein under no. A-59162/2001 dated 14.03.2001, no. A-59164/2001 dated 14.03.2001 and no. L- 16429/1997 dated 06.06.1997.

5. It is submitted that Sh. Hakim Mulkraj Kapoor Talib establish a firm Himalaya Chemical Pharmacy, Hardwar, U.P registered at U.P., Lucknow with board of Indian Medicines dated 22.02.1942. The address of firm kept changing for last 60 years. Now under the name Himalaya Chemical Laboratory Pharmacy with address Hardwar manufacturing medicine for last 40 years. The predecessor of defendant is manufacturing the medicine since the year 1955 under the trademark UJALA in respect of Unani eye drops since the year 1974. The defendant is prior honest user and in prior adoption of the trademark. The defendant has referred to trademark UJALA in Class 5 vide application no. 730634, application no. 725151, application no. 1356947, application no. 854631, application no. 883230 out of which the first and the third are registered and the second and last two are pending registration. It is submitted that Sh. Hakim Mulkraj Kapoor has introduced first time the treatment of eye for Motia Bind ailment which was not covered under the Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940 until amendment dated 01.02.1969 which came into effect on 01.02.1983. The defendant had obtained the licence for the first time under no. U-1255 of 1985 but due to oversight the licence could not renewed. The licence was continued again vide U-1731 of 1989 which is a new licence. It is submitted that the goods are packed by the defendant under distinctive colour of yellow, red and white, style, get up and composition for more than a decade shows that the product originating from defendant alone. Defendant is also using leaflet for marketing of its medicine which include history, composition, usage and other relevant material. The leaflet is artistic, distinctive, has attractive marks and device with peculiar colour scheme, get up, lay out, writing style and arrangement of letters which are registered under Copy Right Act, 1957 as detailed under para no. 4 above. The defendant had advertised the mark in Punjab Kesari dated 25.07.1976, in newspaper Hawk Hardwar on 01.09.1979, a newspaper Mukth Mod dated 01.03.1980, in magazine Blitz (Hindi) dated 19.10.1985, in the year 1986 a Souvenir by U.P Postal Sports Control Board, Government of India, Ministry of Communication, Department of Post shows that defendants are user prior in time for than 20 years before July 1996. In fact the plaintiff has copied the logo of defendant. Plaintiff has got the licence to manufacture drug in the year 1995 and 1996 only. The defendant is showing documents of advertisement since the year 1976 which defendants are using since last 40 years. The defendant has served notice dated 28.04.1997 on the plaintiff to cease and desist from using that Trade Mark UJALA which is not replied by the plaintiff on which reminder notice dated 09.05.1997 was given which was replied on 11.05.1997. Another notice dated 16.05.1997 was served on the defendants reiterating facts of notice dated 28.04.1997 and 09.05.1997. The plaintiff has filed the present suit only after receipt of notice from the defendant.

6. It is submitted by the defendant that they are not proprietor of Mark UJALA and started using it very recently in relation to eye drops. The defendant has achieved the name and reputation by manufacturing eye drops for over 40 years and plaintiff is passing off his goods as of the defendant. Plaintiff is also guilty of copying label of the defendant. The user of plaintiff is fraudulent, ill-motivated and dishonest. It is submitted that plaintiff has appropriated entire get up, design, colour scheme, lay out, letter style and the mark UJALA of the defendant. 13. Issue-wise findings are as follows:

ISSUE NO. 1

Whether this Court has necessary jurisdiction to entertain the present suit?

14. The burden of proof of the present issue was on the defendant. The plaintiff has filed the suit pleading that it is a company duly incorporated under the Indian Companies Act, 1956 having its registered office at E-27, Latowali, Kankhal, Hardwar-249408, U.P. and the same address is also mentioned in the title of the plaint.

14.1 It is matter of judicial notice that registration of trademark for the Northern Region is being done at Delhi and it is settled law that mere registration at Delhi will not confer jurisdiction between the parties at Delhi.

14.2 According to plaintiff, the defendant is using UJALA logo in his business of manufacturing and marketing medicinal and Unani preparation which has striking resemblance to the trademark of plaintiff. It is submitted by the plaintiff at para no. 13 of the plaint that defendant is selling his goods in Delhi and other parts of the country. According to plaintiff cause of action has arose with him on 28.04.1997 when he issued a legal notice to the defendant and the cause of action is continuous in nature.

14.3 At para no. 17 of the plaint it is submitted by the plaintiff that the defendant works for gain in Delhi and the acts of passing off are carried out at Delhi. However plaintiff has failed to mention that how the defendant works for gain at Delhi when in title of the plaint itself plaintiff has mentioned the business activity of defendant at Kharkhani, Hardwar-249401(U.P) and administrative office at Karmagala, Bangalore. In reply to the same it is submitted by the defendant that the defendants are established at Hardwar and their administrative office is at Bangalore.

14.4 The citation from Hon'ble High Court of Delhi titled Ultra Home Construction Pvt. Ltd. v. Purushottam Kumar Chaubey & Ors. on 20 th January 2016 in FAO (OS) 494/2015 & CM 17816/2015 in relevant para no. 13, 14, 15, 16, 17 and 18 is reproduced as under:

"13. It is evident from the above observations that the interpretation given to the expression "carries on business" in the context of a defendant under section 20 of the Code has also been employed in the context of a plaintiff under the said sections 134(2) and 62(2). Thus, in addition to the places where suits could be filed under section 20 of the Code, the plaintiff can also institute a suit under the Trade Marks Act, 1999 and the Copyright Act, 1957, as the case may be, by taking advantage of the provisions of section 134(2) or section 62(2), respectively. Both the latter provisions are in pari materia. Under these provisions four situations can be contemplated in the context of the plaintiff being a corporation (which includes a company). First of all, is the case where the plaintiff has a sole office. In such a case, even if the cause of action has arisen at a different place, the plaintiff can institute a suit at the place of the sole office. Next is the case where the plaintiff has a principal office at one place and a subordinate or branch office at another place and the cause of action has arisen at the place of the principal office. In such a case, the plaintiff may sue at the place of the principal office but cannot sue at the place of the subordinate office. The third case is where the plaintiff has a principal office at one place and the cause of action has arisen at the place where its subordinate office is located. In this eventuality, the plaintiff would be deemed to carry on business at the place of his subordinate office and not at the place of the principal office. Thus, the plaintiff could sue at the place of the subordinate office and cannot sue (under the scheme of the provisions of section 134(2) and 62(2)) at the place of the principal office. The fourth case is where the cause of action neither arises at the place of the principal office nor at the place of the subordinate office but at some other place. In this case, the plaintiff would be deemed to carry on business at the place of its principal office and not at the place of the subordinate office. And, consequently, it could institute a suit at the place of its principal office but not at the place of its subordinate office. All these four cases are set out in the table below for greater clarity:

S.No. Place of Place of Place where Place where Plaintiff's Plaintiff's cause of Plaintiff can Principal Office Subordinate action arose additionally sue (Sole office in /Branch under section s.no.1) Office 134(2) and section 62(2) 1 A -- C A 2 A B A A 3 A B B B 4 A B C A

14. The present case falls under S. No. 3 in the above table. The appellant / plaintiff has its principal office in Delhi (place A). Its subordinate office is at Deogarh, Jharkhand (place B) where it runs one of its hotels (Amrapali Clarks Inn), albeit in collaboration with Clarks Inn hotels. The alleged cause of action has accrued at Deogarh, Jharkhand (place B). Therefore, the appellant/plaintiff could, in respect of the alleged cause of action in this suit, institute a suit against the defendants at Deogarh, Jharkhand (place B) but not in Delhi. Thus, the conclusion arrived at by the learned single judge that this court did not have the territorial jurisdiction to entertain the suit cannot be faulted.

15. We may point out that the learned counsel for the appellant/plaintiff had also urged that there was some conflict between the decisions of the Supreme Court in Dhodha House (supra) and Sanjay Dalia (supra). But, that argument is not available to the learned counsel for multiple reasons. The first and only reason that we may allude to is that in Sanjay Dalia (supra) itself, the Supreme Court, considered its decision in Dhodha House (supra) and held that there was nothing in the interpretation put forth in Sanjay Dalia (supra) which militated against the observations / conclusions in Dhodha House. The exact language used by the Supreme Court on this aspect is as under:-

"42. In Dhodha House [Dhodha House v. S.K. Maingi, (2006) 9 SCC 41] , the question which is posed before us did not come up for consideration and it has been observed that the Act provides for an additional forum. Such additional forum was provided so as to enable a plaintiff who may not be otherwise in a position to file a suit at different places where the copyright was violated. We have anxiously considered the aforesaid observations made and the object of the Act, we find that the interpretation made by us does not militate against the observations made by this Court in Dhodha House [Dhodha House v. S.K. Maingi, (2006) 9 SCC 41] , the precise question which is before us, was not involved in the aforesaid case. A decision is not to be construed like a statute nor by inferential process can it be assumed that this Court has decided the question also which is before this Court in the instant cases."

16. Interestingly, in the present case, even if the deeming fiction of the Explanation in section 20 of the Code is not imported into either of the said sections 134(2) or 62(2), on the basis of the averments made in the plaint itself it becomes clear that the plaintiff carries on business at, inter alia, Deogarh. In Dhodha House (supra) itself it was observed as follows:

"46. The expression "carries on business" and the expression "personally works for gain" connote two different meanings. For the purpose of carrying on business only presence of a man at a place is not necessary. Such business may be carried on at a place through an agent or a manager or through a servant. The owner may not even visit that place. The phrase "carries on business" at a certain place would, therefore, mean having an interest in a business at that place, a voice in what is done, a share in the gain or loss and some control thereover xxxxx" (underlining added)

17. The appellant / plaintiff has a clear and admitted interest in the business of its hotel - Amrapali Clarks Inn - at Deogarh. On the basis of the averments in the plaint, the plaintiff carries on business through the said hotel at Deogarh. To recapitulate, the plaintiff had stated the following in paragraph 39 of the plaint:

"The plaintiff's has long back presence in the town of Deogarh (Jharkhand) where the defendants have launched the impugned project AMBAPALI GREEN. The plaintiff has its hotel in Deogarh which is operational and running in profit and plaintiff's trademark has been known to the local residents and tourists who throng to that place for worship." (underlining added)

18. Clearly, on the basis of the averments in the plaint itself and in the backdrop of the observations in Dhodha House (supra), the appellant / plaintiff carries on business in Deogarh, Jharkhand. It may also carry on business at Delhi. But, because the cause of action has allegedly arisen in Deogarh, Jharkhand, and not in Delhi, the appellant/plaintiff cannot sue the defendants/respondents in Delhi in view of the decision in Sanjay Dalia (supra). Thus, this court does not have the territorial jurisdiction to entertain the suit. And, on this count, the decision of the learned single judge cannot be faulted. But, the suit ought not to have been dismissed. As this court did not have jurisdiction, the plaint ought to have been returned under order 7 rule 10 CPC. Only to that extent, the learned single judge had erred."

14.5 It is admitted as correct by PW-1 that plaintiff company has no branch office in Delhi. However plaintiff company has sale of his product at Delhi. The document of plaintiff are Ex.PW1/2 to Ex.PW1/13. The invoice of plaintiff Ex.PW1/2 to Ex.PW1/9 are showing address of Hardwar, U.P. The plaintiff needs to show that the defendant is selling his products at Delhi. Mere registration of trademark at Delhi being the office for the entire Northern Region will not confer jurisdiction on the parties to file suit at Delhi. The plaintiff must show that it has office in Delhi. Plaintiff has admitted that he has no branch office at Delhi. Plaintiff has admittedly registered office at Hardwar, U.P. Thus the plaintiff neither has branch office nor has its Principal office at Delhi. The plaintiff seeks to rely on the fact that defendant is carrying its business at Delhi which is denied by the defendant. The onus of proof of the above averments is on the plaintiff. It is for the plaintiff to show that defendant is so carrying its business at Delhi on face of such denial by the defendant. All the documents filed by the plaintiff which are invoice exhibiting sale of products are pertaining to the plaintiff.

14.6 Hence the plaintiff must show that the defendant has its office at Delhi. Plaintiff has not filed any document to show the user of its trademark by the defendant at its office at Delhi. The invoices filed by the defendant Ex.DW1/4(colly) page 33 to 36 shows that vide bill no. 7851 goods are sold to Jupiter Publicity Company at New Delhi and also to Capsons Printer vide bill no. 007649 dated 18.10.1993 at Bazar Sita Ram, Delhi. The said goods are sold by the defendant to its customers at Delhi. In such case, when cause of action has arisen at different place, then the plaintiff can institute a suit at the place of its sole office and not at the place where cause of action has allegedly arose. The said law is laid down in case titled Ultra Home Constructions Pvt. Ltd. v. Purushottam Kumar Chaubey (supra). Hence, even when the goods of defendant are sold at Delhi the plaintiff does not has jurisdiction to institute suit at Delhi in absence of its Principal or branch office at Delhi. For showing of cause of action in favour of plaintiff at Delhi in the present case the plaintiff must show that it has branch office at Delhi. Plaintiff has admitted vide PW-1 in cross examination dated 10.08.2009 at page 4 that it has no branch office at Delhi. However plaintiff has deposed that it has sale of product at Delhi. Mere sale of product by plaintiff or defendant at Delhi does not confer jurisdiction before the present Court in favour of the plaintiff. Thereby the plaintiff can sue the defendant only at its Principal office and not even at its branch office when the cause of action has not arisen at branch office. The plaintiff can sue at branch office only if cause of action has arisen at its branch office. In the present case the plaintiff does not have either the Principal office or its branch office at Delhi. Thereby merely on the basis of cause of action of selling of goods to its customers at Delhi by the plaintiff or selling of goods by the defendant at Delhi cannot allow plaintiff to institute the suit at Delhi.

14.7 Hence, it is held that plaintiff does not have jurisdiction to institute the present case before the present Court and suit of the plaintiff is held barred by jurisdiction. Hence, the suit of the plaintiff is returned to be filed before the Court of competent jurisdiction.
