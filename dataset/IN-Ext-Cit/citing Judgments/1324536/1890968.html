The present appeal has been filed against judgment of conviction and order of sentence dated 20.10.2003, vide which the present appellant-accused was convicted for offences punishable under Sections 302 and 324 of the Indian Penal Code (hereinafter to be referred as `IPC') and was sentenced to undergo life imprisonment and to pay fine of Rs.5000/- and in default of payment of fine to further undergo rigorous imprisonment for three months for offence punishable under Section 302 Crl.A.No.886-DB of 2003 -2- IPC and he was further sentenced to undergo rigorous imprisonment for a period of one year and to pay a fine of Rs.1000/- and in dafault of payment of fine, to further undergo rigorous imprisonment for one month for offence punishable under Section 324 IPC. However, both the substantive sentences were ordered to run concurrently. It was also ordered that out of amount of fine, if recovered, Rs.5000/- would be paid to Pritam Dass, complainant

2. Briefly stated, the case of prosecution is that Pritam Dass, complainant was resident of Indhna Kalaske, Police Station Goraya, District Jalandhar. He was a carpenter. They were three brothers, eldest being Garib Dass, who used to reside in Delhi. Nazar Ram is younger to Garib Dass and older to Pritam Dass, complainant. Pritam Dass has two sons and one daughter. 3. On 25.10.2001, at about 7.00 p.m., Paramjit- accused was abusing his father Nazar Ram and was asking him to leave the house. Complainant and his sons heard the noise and they also came in the common courtyard, where the accused and his father Nazar Ram were standing. Complainant and his sons Yogesh Chander and Sukhwinder asked Paramjit to stop abusing his father and also asked him as to why he was asking his father to leave the house. Accused gave another sua blow on the left side of chest of Sukhwinder (deceased) due to which he fell down. Pritam Dass and his sons raised alarm. 22. Learned trial Court convicted and sentenced the accused, as aforementioned against which the present appeal has been filed.

23. We have heard learned counsel for the appellant-accused and learned Additional Advocate General, Punjab and have gone through the whole record carefully.

24. It has been argued by learned counsel for the appellant that there is inordinate delay in lodging the first information report, hence, no reliance can be placed upon the testimony of prosecution witnesses. It has further been argued that the prosecution has failed to explain the injury on the person of the accused and the said fact also creates doubt in the verasity of the prosecution story. It is also contended that both the eye-witnesses examined by the prosecution are closely related to the deceased and hence, interested one and cannot be said to be independent witnesses. It has also been argued that there was no motive on the part of the accused to have committed murder of his cousin Sukhwinder.

25. On the other hand, it has been argued by learned Additional Advocate General, Punjab, that delay in lodging the first information report has been duly explained hence, no doubt is created in the story of the prosecution witnesses. It is further contended that the eye-witnesses are the most natural witnesses, as admittedly complainant's family as well as the family of the accused being closely related, used to reside in the same house and having common courtyard and hence, their testimony cannot be Crl.A.No.886-DB of 2003 -10- discarded merely on the ground that they are closely related to the deceased. It is further contended that the injury on the person of the accused was self suffered, just to create defence and that there was also motive for the accused to have caused injury to his cousin Yogesh Chander and Sukhwinder as they and their father used to tell the accused that he should not abuse his father and that he should not ask his father to leave the house without any reason.

26. So far as delay in lodging the first information report is concerned, there is no dispute that the occurrence had taken place on 25.10.2001 at about 7.00 p.m. and, however, the matter was reported to the police on 26.10.2001 at about 12.15 p.m. and hence, there is delay of more than 17 hours. However, it is settled law that case of prosecution cannot be thrown merely on the ground of delay in reporting the matter to the police, if the delay is properly explained. The occurrence had taken place in the night time. Accused had run away from the house after causing injury to both of his cousins resulting into death of Sukhwinder. It has been deposed by Pritam Dass as well as by Yogesh Chander, PWs that they were frightened and they did not go out of the house due to fear of the accused. Hence, complainant preferred to go to the police on the next morning. Police station was situated at a distance of about 7 kms from the village, where occurrence had taken place. However, when complainant was proceeding to police Station for reporting the matter to the police, the Sub Inspector met him on the way and he recorded his statement at 12.15 p.m. Moreover the complainant as well as accused are closely related and belongs to the same family, hence, it cannot be said that delay in lodging the report with the police in this case is fatal to the case of prosecution. Crl.A.No.886-DB of 2003 -11- Learned counsel has rightly placed reliance on Ravinder Kumar v. State of Punjab (SC) 2001 Crl.L.J.4242, relevant paragraphs of which read as under:

"14. When there is criticism of the ground that FIR in a case was delayed the Court has to look at the reason why there was such a delay. There can be a variety of genuine causes for FIR lodgment to get delayed. Rural people might be ignorant of the need for informing the police of a crime without any lapse of time. This kind of unconversantness is not too uncommon among urban people also. They might not immediately think of going to the police station. Another possibility is due to lack of adequate transport facilities for the informers to reach the police station. The third, which is a quite common bearing, is that the kith and kin of deceased might take some appreciable time to regain a certain level of tranquility of mind or sedativeness of temper for moving to the police station for the purpose of furnishing the requisite information. Yet another cause is, the persons who are supposed to give such information themselves could be physically impaired that the police had to reach them on getting some nebulous information about the incident.
15. We are not providing an exhausting catalogue of instances which could cause delay in lodging the FIR. Our effort is to try to point out the stale demand made in the criminal courts to treat the FIR vitiated merely on the ground of delay in its lodgment cannot be approved as a legal corollary. In any case, where there is delay in making the FIR the court is to look at the causes are not attributable to any effort to concoct a version no consequence shall be attached to the mere delay in lodging the FIR. Vide Zahoor v. State of U.P. 1991 Supl.(1) SCC 372; Tara Singh v. State of Punjab 1991 Suppl.(1) SCC 536; Jamna v. State of U.P. 1994 (1) SCC 185. In Tara Singh (supra) made the following Crl.A.No.886-DB of 2003 -12- observations:
`It is well settled that the delay in giving the FIR by itself cannot be ground to doubt the prosecution case. Knowing the Indian conditions as they are we cannot except these villagers to rush to the police station immediately after the occurrence. Human nature as it is, the kith and kin who have witnessed the occurrence cannot be expected to act mechanically with all the promptidue in giving the report to the police. At times being grief stricken because of the calamity it may be immediately occur to them that they should give a report. After all it is but natural in these circumstances for them to take some time to go to the police station for giving the report.'"

27. So far as argument of learned counsel for the accused that both the eye witnesses are closely related to the deceased and hence no reliance should be placed on their testimony, the same is also devoid of any merit. Testimony of Pritam Dass-PW7 and Yogesh Chander- PW8, who was also injured in the occurrence, is consistent on all the material points. They are the most natural witnesses of the occurrence, as they along with family of the accused used to reside in the same house and it was but natural for them to have come out of their house in the common courtyard of the house where accused was abusing his father and was telling him to go out of the house.

28. There is no reason as to why Pritam Dass and his son Yogesh Chander should have falsely implicated the accused in this case and as to why they should have allowed the real culprit to escape from the criminal liability. No material discrepancy has come in the statements of PW7

-Pritam Dass and PW8-Yogesh Chander. There are only minor discrepancies on the basis of which no doubt is created in the verasity of Crl.A.No.886-DB of 2003 -13- these witnesses. Deposition of these witnesses is also duly corroborated by medical evidence, as detailed above. On this point reliance is placed upon State of Punjab vs.Hari Singh and another 1974 CU.L.J. 307, wherein it was observed as under:

"10.----- It has been repeatedly held by this Court that the mere fact a witness is related to the victim could not be a good enough ground for rejecting his testimony although it may be a ground for scrutinizing his evidence more critically and carefully where facts and circumstances disclose that only relations have been produced and others presumably independent witnesses, were available, were not produced. In the instant case, there is no evidence that anyone besides the witnesses produced had actually witnessed the attack upon Gian Singh and Zora Singh."

29. In the present case as well no evidence has come on the record that anyone else besides the witnesses produced, had actually witnessed the attack upon the deceased and the witness Yogesh Chander, except Nazar Ram, who is none else than the father of the accused himself, who was rightly given up by the prosecution as having been won over by the accused.

30. In Dalip Singh and others v. The State of Punjab, AIR 1953 SC 364, it was held by Hon'ble Apex Court that interested, related witnesses would not like to involve innocent person by leaving aside the real culprits. So far as the minor discrepancies in the deposition of prosecution witnesses is concerned, reliance is placed upon State of Tamilnadu vs. Karuppusamy and ors. 1992 CAR 339 (SC), wherein it was observed by Hon'ble Apex Court that only tutored witnesses deposed in a parrot like fashion and that a natural witness is bound to commit some Crl.A.No.886-DB of 2003 -14- mistakes.

31. So far as injury on the person of the accused is concerned, it has been specifically deposed by both the eye-witnesses that they did not cause injury to the accused. However, from the medical evidence adduced by the accused, it is proved that he was having a simple injury on his person as deposed by Dr.Kewal Verma, DW1. However, accused has failed to prove the plea taken by him in his statement under Section 313 Cr.P.C., as to the manner in which he sustained the said injury. Even his father did not appear in the witness box to corroborate his version taken in his statement under Section 313 Cr.P.C. The Medical Officer specifically deposed in the cross-examination that the injury on the person of Paramjit could be self inflicted. It has been deposed by the Medical Officer that he had advised X-ray examination and however, X-ray report was not shown to him which shows that accused had sustained only simple injury on the forearm. Hence, learned trial Court has rightly observed that the injury on the person of accused was self suffered in order to create defence in his favour and has rightly placed reliance upon Rizan and another vs. State of Chhatisgarh through the Chief Secretary, Govt. of Chhatisgarh, Raipur AIR 2003 Supreme Court 976. Relevant paragraph of the same reads as under:

"14. The number of injuries is not always a safe criterion for determining who the aggressor was. It cannot be stated as a universal rule that whenever the injuries are on the body of the accused persons, a presumption must necessarily be raised that the accused persons had caused injuries in exercise of the right of private defence. The defence has to further establish that the injuries so caused on the accused probabilises the version of the right of private defence. Non-explanation of the injuries sustained by the Crl.A.No.886-DB of 2003 -15- accused at about the time of occurrence or in the course of altercation is a very important circumstance. But mere non- explanation of the injuries by the prosecution may not affect the prosecution case in all cases. This principle applies to cases where the injuries sustained by the accused are minor and superficial or where the evidence is so clear and cogent, so independent and disinterested, so probable, consistent and creditworthy, that it far outweighs the effect of the omission on the part of the prosecution to explain the injuries. .........."

32. Hence, there is no force in the argument of learned counsel for the accused that due to non-explanation of one simple injury on the person of the accused, the prosecution version becomes doubtful.

33. So far as the argument of learned counsel for the accused that there was no motive for the offence is concerned, it is settled law that in cases where independent testimony is available to prove the murder charge against the accused, the question of motive becomes more or less academic. For this view reliance is placed on Podda Narayana and others vs. State of Andhra Pradesh AIR 1975 Supreme Court 1252. Relevant paragraph of the same reads as under:

"13. The learned Sessions Judge further held that the motive ascribed to the appellants for committing the murder of the deceased was not sufficient to impel them to plan the murder of the deceased. This finding of the learned Sessions Judge is based purely on speculation. Various persons react to circumstances in different ways and it is difficult to weigh the reaction of the persons in golden scales with absolute computerised accuracy. There is no doubt that the deceased had drawn the accused in a long litigation involving thousands of rupees as a result of which he had to attend the Court at Anantpur on various dates. The sequence of circumstances under which the deceased was murdered clearly shows that Crl.A.No.886-DB of 2003 -16- there could not have been any other motive but the institution of the suit. The High Court has also pointed out that the prosecution has established good and sufficient motive for the murder of the deceased. Furthermore, in view of the independent testimony of P.Ws. 1, 2 and 3 whom the High Court has believed, and we see no reason to differ from the view of the High Court, the question of motive becomes more or less academic. On this point also, in our opinion, the learned Additional Sessions Judge has taken an absolutely wrong view. We have been taken through the entire evidence of P.Ws. 1, 2 and 3 who are independent witnesses and against whom no animus has been established by the accused and we do not see any reason to disbelieve their evidence. The High Court, therefore, rightly believed their evidence in order to accept the prosecution case. We do not find any error of law in the approach made by the High Court."

34. In the present case it has come into evidence that the complainant and his sons, i.e., Yogesh Chander and Sukhwinder (deceased) used to object the accused for abusing his father Nazar Ram on earlier occasions as well. Hence, on the day of occurrence when the complainant and his sons objected to the accused as to why he was abusing his father and as to why he was asking him to go out of the house without any reason, he got enraged, went inside the house and brought out a Sua with which he first caused injury to PW Yogesh which hit him on his arm and he gave another injury to Sukhwinder (deceased) in his chest which proved fatal. Hence, it cannot be said that injuries were caused by the accused to the deceased and his brother without any motive.

35. On this point reliance has also been placed upon Krishna Pillai Sree Kumar and another vs. State of Kerala 1981 A.I.R. (SC) 1237, wherein it has been observed that sometimes murders are known to have Crl.A.No.886-DB of 2003 -17- actuated by much lesser motives. Relevant paragraph 7 of the judgment reads as under:

"7. It is undisputed that some bad blood existed between the deceased on the one hand and the appellants on the other prior to the occurrence. The animosity may not have been very bitter but then it is too much to say that it could not possibly form a motive for the occurrence. The variation in human nature being so vast murders are known to have been actuated by much lesser motives. In any case, it is not a sine qua non for the success of the prosecution that the motive must be proved. So long as the other evidence remains convincing and is not open to reasonable doubt, a conviction may well be based on it."

36. As already discussed above, admittedly occurrence had taken place in the courtyard of the house, which was common to the complainant as well as to the accused. Accused had also taken the plea in his statement under Section 313 Cr.P.C. that the complainant and his son Yogesh Chander had come and caused injuries to him and that Sukhwinder received injuries at their hands when he intervened. However, accused has failed to prove the said plea. Even father of the accused did not appear in the witness box to support his plea. No other member in his family has also been examined in defence. The plea taken is also a vague one. Even it has not been mentioned as to whether they came armed with any weapon in their hands. Hence, learned trial Court rightly came to the conclusion that accused caused injury with Sua first to PW Yogesh Chander and then to Sukhwinder resulting into his death.

37 It has further been argued by learned counsel for the accused that even if prosecution evidence is believed, no offence punishable under Crl.A.No.886-DB of 2003 -18- Section 302 IPC is made out against him and at the most the offence made out is under Section 304 IPC. It is argued that there was no previous enmity between prosecution witnesses and the Sukhwinder (deceased) on the one hand, and the accused on the other hand.

38. He has also placed reliance upon Muthu v.State by Inspector of Police, Tamil Nadu (SC) 2007(4) R.C.R. (Criminal) 919; Shiva Karam Payaswami Tewari v. State of Maharashtra (SC) 2009 (1) R.C.R. (Criminal) 877; Sripathi and others v. State of Karnataka (SC) 2009 (2) R.C.R. (Criminal) 597; and Satish Narayan Sawant v. State of Goa 2009 (4) RCR (Criminal) 327.

39. On the other hand, it has been argued by learned Additional Advocate General, Punjab, that the aforementioned authorities are not applicable on the facts of the present case and that in this case intention of the accused to commit murder of Sukhwinder can be gathered from the fact that he went inside his house, brought out a Sua and caused injury first to Yogesh Chander PW and then Sukhwinder (deceased) on vital part of the body, i.e., in the chest.

40. We do not find any merit in the submission of the learned counsel for the accused on the point, rather submission of learned Additional Advocate General, Punjab is having force. Complainant Pritam Dass along with both his sons Yogesh Chander-PW8 and Sukhwinder (deceased) had come in the courtyard of their house when they heard accused abusing his father Nazar Ram and telling him to leave the house. Prosecution witnesses were closely related to Nazar Ram and hence, it was but natural for them to have come out of the house and asked the accused as to why he was abusing his father. In this case injury was not caused to Crl.A.No.886-DB of 2003 -19- prosecution witnesses by the accused there and then, rather he went inside his house and brought out a weapon, i.e., Sua. He first caused injury to Yogesh Chander PW and however, the same did not hit on his vital part and the same hit only on his arm. He did not stop there and caused fatal injury to Sukhwinder in his chest. Receipt of injury on the body of Sukhwinder, nature of weapon used in causing the injury, and the manner in which injury was caused clearly show that accused had the requisite intention to commit murder of Sukhwinder.

41. On this point reliance has also been placed on Suraj Bhan vs. State of Haryana 2003(1) RCR (Criminal) 250, wherein accused had given one lathi blow with force on head of deceased causing fracture and death and, however, accused was held guilty of offence under Section 302 IPC. Relevant paragraph 9 of the judgment reads as under:

"9. Learned counsel then argued that since the appellant has dealt only a single blow, the offence if at all, cannot be the one falling under Section 302 IPC or at the most, it would come under Section 304, Part II, IPC since there is absolutely on material to show that the appellant had any knowledge that he would be causing an imminent death of the deceased. In this regard we have examined the medical evidence and the manner in which the assault in question has taken place. The doctor has opined that the injury was caused in such a manner as to cause the death of the deceased which on dissection found by the doctor, had caused a fracture of the left parietal bone causing extra dural haemotoma. The doctor has opined that the death was due to shock and haemorrhage and as a result of the head injury which was sufficient to cause death in the ordinary course of nature. We are of the opinion that the appellant must be attributed with the knowledge that when he used a lathi forcefully on the head of a person, he was likely to cause death Crl.A.No.886-DB of 2003 -20- of the said person, the prosecution had also proved that this appellant had the intention to kill the deceased, therefore, we have no hesitation in rejecting the argument of learned counsel on this count also."

42. In the present case the injury with a sharp edged weapon was caused in the chest of deceased by the accused and as per deposition of PW2-Dr.Ram Parshad Verma, the wound passed through the tissues of the 3rd inter costal space and entered in the upper part of thorax cavity passing through the substances of upper lobe of left lung and that the left chest cavity was full of dark clotted blood and that his death was caused due to shock and haemorrhage resulting from the said injury. Hence, prosecution has been able to prove that the appellant-accused had the intention to kill the deceased. Hence, the trial Court has rightly convicted the accused for offences under Sections 302 and 324 IPC, and sentenced him to undergo life imprisonment, besides other sentences, as aforementioned.

43. No other point was urged.

44. We do not find any merit in the appeal. Hence, the same is, hereby, dismissed.
